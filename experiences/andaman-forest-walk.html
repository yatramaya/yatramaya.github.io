Andaman Forest Walk

Timeline : 2014 April
India Asia Half Day Forest Beach

Neil Island, being one of the smaller ones of the Andaman archipelago, is an inconspicuous dot in the vast blue waters of the mighty Indian Ocean. Here one gets the sensation of being ‘bound’ or ’limited’ by the land area. Getting to experience both sunrise and sunset on the same day was something that I looked forward to on small islands and Neil had not disappointed me in that respect. In just a couple of days of arriving on Neil Island, I got the feeling that we had already explored everything there was worth exploring. So when my local host offered to take us to a ‘secret’ beach the next morning, I could not contain my excitement and curiosity. A ‘secret’ beach! Where could that possibly be?

We set off at dawn after a breakfast that I insisted on being mostly consisting of fruit because I expected a long walk. We were accompanied by Shrivas and his three enthusiastic boys. Initially it was an unmarked trail that took us through neighbouring coconut farms where at one point a “tender coconut break” was declared. While the coconut trees were only about two persons tall, I still wondered about what implements would be needed for us to reach towards the refreshing fruit. That was when one of the boys, with the dexterity of a trained climber climbed onto one of the trees and started dropping coconuts. This was also when Shrivas revealed his preparedness when he removed a machete from the little bag he was carrying and just like that, we were all sipping the sweetest and coolest tender coconut water; energy that should help us on our second leg of the journey.

I noticed the foliage on the forest floor around the same time that I noticed the lack of harsh sun burning a hole into my neck. Our walk had progressed from the cultivated sections to the well wooded areas with trees so tall that I couldn’t estimate the height of their canopies. My host was animatedly gesturing at the size of some of the trees and I couldn’t agree more. They were mammoth in size. They were estimated to be over 500 years old, he told me and I noticed that age and wisdom does reveal itself on their barks. They had fissures so deep and wide that stems of other medium trees could be easily tucked into them.

I moved forward with carefully placed, measured steps as the forest floor seemed full of life. But even then my nimble footed attempts would break a twig here or crush a leaf there and I would look up and check if I had in any way disturbed the great-grandmother trees that were having an afternoon nap. In fact if it wasn’t for our watches, the lack of penetrating sunlight would have fooled me into thinking that it was late evening.

It was not dark everywhere though. Once in a while we came across openings that were created by large trees that had fallen and younger ones in the area were competing for a spot; a process that should take them many more years if they were spared the axe by us. The fallen trees had completely cut off the trail in some places and we had to take detours. Sometimes we could climb over the rotting trunks that were now homes to various insects and reptiles that were seeking their meals. At this point it seemed that we were the only source of sound in the forest. While the boys walked ahead with the inherent curiosity of childhood, we followed along with Shrivas behind.

Shrivas spoke to me continuously about a lot of things, but distracted as I was, in a silent communion with the trees, I registered only parts of it. He spoke of the times his father migrated to the Andamans during the conflict in East Pakistan (now Bangladesh) and the ensuing refugee crisis. About how these islands had a larger number of trees and undoubtedly a large number of animals, including mammals. Sadly, only nominal portions of that past glory now remains.

Somewhere up in the canopy I heard muffled sounds. It must have been birds although I did not hear any calling. I craned my neck and made a futile attempt to look through the dense foliage well knowing that it was an impossibility to spot anything. And perhaps to relieve myself of the tragic knowledge of the history and destiny of these beautiful forests at our hands, I imagined that the long vines would come to life and like tentacles pick us up and throw us out of their territory.

I was unsure if it was my anticipation but the fresh aroma of the earth underneath the forest had a tinge of saltiness to it. I couldn’t help thinking, almost wishing, that the sea must be close by despite there being no sounds of waves. I eagerly awaited for a dramatic event of the forest thinning out to reveal the blues and the whites of sandy beaches but instead the forest thickened and we seemed to be going deeper. Shrivas however, assured me that the walk was short and we would be soaking up the afternoon sun in no time. To make most out of the time in the forest, I stopped and took photographs of the creatures I met. Crabs, spiders and insects of various shapes and colours had made homes everywhere under our feet.

Larger animals such as the wild boar and deer now existed only in the stories that Shrivas was telling me as I stopped and sat down to take photographs of trail of ants that were on their own version of a mid-day trek, unminding of our giant legs, marching along fallen twigs, stones and precariously perched dry leaves. The sudden darkening of the surroundings was a cue for me to continue on the walk.

Just when I had let myself focus on the goals ahead and had started to walk briskly, a movement on the forest floor forced me to pause again. It was a large crab. It was larger than any I had ever seen before. But despite my best intentions and stealth, it ran away into the nearest hole in the ground before I could get my camera out. But all was not lost. A chameleon which sat at a vantage point and was observing my madness was willing to pose for a photograph.

My host was amused that I was even spotting these creatures. In fact he proudly declared that his skills at spotting was exclusively used when he needed to eat them. Then he went ahead joked and congratulated me for having the skills for survival. While we shared that laugh, his sons who found my interest rather amusing volunteered to spot creepy crawlies. This made the rest of the walk even more enjoyable.

When we finally reached the sea, the drama that I wished for wasn’t as I had expected. Instead of being visible as a vast nothingness from afar to seduce us as the forest ended, it was as if a wooded curtain had been drawn ahead of us to hide the beach. We cut through the foliage and vines and then stepped over knotty exposed roots to find ourselves all of a sudden out in the open, on a sandy beach with clear blue water and blue skies for the backdrop. It had taken only about 45 minutes to this secluded beach but it felt like we walked for hours.

When I looked to the left it was the beach for a few hundred meters and and then the land curved behind us. To the right I could see that the beach ended abruptly after which the land met the sea at a series of large rocks. All along the shore smaller trees and mangroves lined up like a margin between the sand and the old forest.

While we walked towards the right end of the beach, unable to sustain the patience that adults seem to have, the boys ran into the water. I climbed one of the rocks that was mostly in the shallow water but provided a nice ramp to climb to a vantage point above the sand. It was beautiful from the top, the endless ocean meeting a white sandy beach and then the lush green of the forest. To know that such a space existed within the touristy Neil island was new knowledge to me. I do not know how much of a ‘secret’ it was and even if so, how long would it remain one.

The boys who weren’t here for the views had already got onto the business of using the rock as a diving platform and were one by one launching themselves into the cool waters below. One of them had also found a makeshift raft, the discovery of which resulted in their own invented games of exploration and overturning. Shrivas meanwhile was calling me down for another side adventure.

Shrivas proposed that we explore a cave to our right, just after the beach had ended and under the high rocks that further formed the shoreline. To reach there, we would have to first enter the water and then wade towards the right. This at first seemed easy considering the water was shallow near the shore, at the most, reaching chest levels. The problem was the strong waves that terminated with a bang against the rocks.

As soon as we started on this mission, I could see why Shrivas had gently broached the topic. The force was unlike what I imagined. The constant waves threatened to knock me off my feet and paste me onto the sharp rocks on my right. It was advised that I move slowly in between other smaller rocks to lessen the impact of the waves. The fact that I had to step on sharp rocks that cut the underside of my feet made this harder than it was supposed to be. I somehow made it back in one piece after taking a u-turn at the cave which was anyway not accessible for any kind of detailed inspection.

I was relieved to be back on the shore after the little adventure that left me drained. After some rest, I decided instead to join the kids at the diving platform. Ensuring that there was enough depth for a dive, I took to the platform for the first dive. Seeing that a long standing invitation was taken, the boys cheered me on and dived in to experience the cool waters. A few more rounds of this activity and some swimming later, we took a break for a snack.

When Shrivas went and slept under the shade of a tree I got the message that it was time for an afternoon siesta. Taking the cue, I proceeded for the same. The children had by now with renewed energy taken to the makeshift raft again and were frolicking in the water. As I closed my eyes, I thought about my good fortune to have visited this quiet corner of Neil island and experiencing the solitude. A gentle breeze caressed me as I dozed off.
