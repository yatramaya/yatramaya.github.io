Trip to a Bolivian Consulate

Timeline : 2019 August
Chile South America Half Day City People

Most of the tourists who land at the Calama Airport in Northern Chile have but one purpose which is to book a direct taxi to their real destination, San Pedro de Atacama, the jewel of the Atacama desert, a picturesque town that is tailored around tourism. Calama, on the other hand is a mining town visited by workers - both short and long term - and people who have some business connected to mining. The city seems flat with mud coloured houses, shops, narrow streets, workshops and the necessary infrastructure to maintain the families of the migrant workers. But Calama had something that we certainly needed; a Bolivian Consulate, the only one apart from the one at Santiago, the capital of Chile. Tucked not very far away from its busy streets and market, we were planning to try our luck there in obtaining a Bolivian Visa a priori which would help us when we eventually crossed over from Chile to Bolivia. When we arrived in Calama it was already the end of the working day, so first we needed a place to rest. Due to very low recreational tourism, good places were hard to find but we did manage to find a decent hostel with a kitchen. A kitchen was a priority because without any reliable vegetarian food available around us, we were keen to cook for ourselves.

What I didn’t expect though was how refreshingly simple and pleasant Calama was. I immediately fell in love with the narrow streets with their graffiti coated walls, the multitudes of small shops, the fresh vegetables-and-fruits and most importantly the complete freedom of not being seen as a “tourist”. Apart from the “any-town” status of Calama what helped was our Indian features including our black hair. If not from Chile itself, we were quite often mistaken to be South Americans; somewhere from the same continent. This often led to hilarious exchanges when someone would rapid-fire me with questions or greetings in the street in Spanish and to avoid being rude upfront, my reaction would be to smile, nod my head or all at the same time for hopelessly too long and then at the end of which I would eventually come to the point when in my broken Spanish I’d said something close to ““lo siento, no hablo Espagnol”, which by the way was the first thing I learnt to say in that language. But with all the Spanish I could not speak, I did manage to shop for some groceries, vegetables and even source some pre-mixed pisco sour to put together a good dinner for ourselves at the hostel.

The next day we decided to walk to the Consulate, about twenty minutes away from our hostel. It was a small building and curiously enough, it felt as if we were entering someone’s house via the garage-door; there wasn’t much of a facade to talk about. Inside, there were five people already waiting. We added ourselves to that list of hopefuls. When a friendly staff politely approached us and inquired about our visit, in broken Spanish but convincingly enough we replied that we wanted to apply for Bolivian Visas. He seemed to understand, as quite understandably it was one of their main functions. It was the queuing up that he was actually amused about. I understood that we had missed a few essential steps. He showed us some sample forms and indicated that we should fill it. As he spoke I registered the words “necessita” “applicacion” “documento” but also realized that the form was not a physical one but an online registration. He was kind enough to give us the details of the website and we promised him we would be back after completing these tasks. I was extremely grateful that he did not wait for us to get to our turn to tell us this. We rushed back to our hostel and started the process of filling in the form, attaching the necessary documents and photos to obtain a printable document with all our details pre-filled in. All we needed now were printouts of all this and we should be set. We looked for a cyber-centre nearby with some help from our friendly hostel staff with whom I had by now, managed to share a reliable grammar, if not a language. Feeling empowered that we were now armed with the all the paperwork we walked into the Consulate yet again, this time with new-found confidence, trading smiles with the now familiar official who had spoken to us the first time. We were asked to wait for our turn.

I had expected this would be a quick process once the form was submitted but it was apparent things were not smooth as yet. The official who was excited to help us initially, asking us about our purpose of visit to Bolivia, duration and such, soon retreated to a corner and was lost on his computer. I noticed his face developed a worried expression as he stared at the screen. A few minutes ago he was accommodating enough to even accept the payment in cash because we happened to look lost when originally instructed to make the payment at a specific bank. I wondered if he was going to come back with yet another task that would send us on the errands around Calama. He then consulted his colleague seated beside him, who in turn started browsing the Internet for something. I was puzzled. With all the details already filled in, what could it be that he was looking for? He called us closer but only for all of us to nod at each other because both parties had emptied their baskets of simple words and phrases. This was something that was beyond street language is what I gathered. He turned his computer screen so that we could see it and started searching for Visa rules for Indian Passport holders. This made me extremely insecure, as the last thing I had expected was to have any problems with the criteria for visa. We waited like that for a few minutes, with nothing happening in particular, looking at the queue getting shorter and the clock threateningly inching closer to their closing time.

At some point our patience was rewarded and I could stop imagining the worst when he smiled as if struck by an epiphany and pulled out his phone. He had the translation app too. The app did not do a great job of translating but it achieved the bare minimum of conveying to us the good news after which all of us were broadly smiling. As it turned out, it wasn’t the visa criteria or even ourselves that were problem. It was the visa fee. They were just not clear about how much to charge for Indians. They started looking through their files for archived forms and informed us that were the first Indians to walk into this particular Consulate for a Visa. So they did not have any references too. While I stood there a little amused and with a tinge of self-importance, he returned to us the dollars we had paid earlier. The verdict was that we did not have to pay anything. The Bolivian Visa was free for us if we obtained it at a Consulate.

They day couldn’t have gotten better but it did. Just then, the Consular General walked into the room, a gentleman who was dressed to look prim and proper and most importantly serious. He spoke to them concisely and purposefully. He then looked at us and spoke to us in English, asking us about our travel. He then told us not to worry and that our visa will be ready in just a few minutes. I was thrilled and thanked him profusely. I told him what a pleasant experience it was visiting the Consulate and how grateful we were for all the help from the friendly officials. He told us that he was a native of La Paz, the capital of Bolivia and told us what a beautiful country Bolivia was. I was now extra-eager to find out for myself, having got a taste of Bolivian hospitality at the Consulate itself, instilling confidence that our decision to visit this small, often ignored, country in South America was going to be very exciting, after all.
