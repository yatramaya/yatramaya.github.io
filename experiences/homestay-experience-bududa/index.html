Staying in a remote East Ugandan Village, Africa

October 15, 2020 3 minutes

Uganda Africa

Multi Day

“Is it true that women will be burnt alive for dowry in India?” Mai asked with her fear in her eyes and her palm covering her mouth. She did not mean to offend me. The innocence in her voice and her genuine concern for her sisters in India made me offer a simple, plain answer. “Yes, it does happen even now in some villages, although it is not legal” I tried to calm her down. “I heard that in India women have to pay dowry money? And not the other way around?” her line of questions on women empowerment or the lack of it continued. Then she shared with me rather proudly the story of how her husband had married her in exchange for four cows and a small piece of land. And so went our conversations till late in the evening while the food slowly cooked on the fire giving out irresistible aroma. I had become part of their family for the seven days I spent at their home. “Mai” means mother in Lugisu, a local language spoken around this region.

My travels had taken me to an inconspicuous village at the foothills of the majestic Mt. Elgon in eastern Uganda. The one time this village was in news was due to a devastating landslide that had occurred a few years ago. Surrounded by lush green mountains, this small village called Bukali was in a valley with a river running across it, there were a few houses with coffee, plantain, vegetables growing abundantly.

I was keen to hear her stories, as much as, learn cooking from her. She could produce the most delicious dishes and make it seem effortless. When I started peeling raw bananas for matoke - a cooked, mashed up raw plantain dish, I realized how difficult it was. We cooked in the courtyard on a firewood stove, under the stars, sipping chilled local beer. Coming from India, I could relate to her stories, many of which drew similarities from people back home. We were both colonized by the British, to begin with. This was also the reason we could converse in a common tongue. Mai explained to me about the population issue here, the unwanted pregnancies, the lack of awareness about HIV, orphaned children, poverty. She told me that one of the children playing around in her courtyard that morning was not her grandchild but an orphan whose father was unknown and the mother died of AIDS. She had generously taken him into her fold and was bringing him up as one of her grandchildren. A shy, mute whom I had met earlier was HIV positive and Mai told me that she had no family, so she would come to work and eat here.

That night I could barely sleep. I was thinking about the cozy life I led, the arrogance and selfishness that inherently comes with affluence. And how here in this small village I was humbled. I was not preached about their way of life or taught human values. Yet, subtly, by living with them, how easily I was considered as one of their own, how they were kind enough to let me into their homes, to share with me their stories openly, trustingly. They believed it to be their duty to take care of others who were lesser fortunate, and share whatever little they have with others. Isn’t this what we call “dharma” back home? At that moment I felt the boundaries blur, the differences fading and a deep bond connecting us. I felt as though I knew them forever. And as though I had met my long lost ancestors.
