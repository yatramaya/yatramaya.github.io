High Altitude Capital City, La Paz, Bolivia
High Altitude Capital City, La Paz, Bolivia

Timeline : 2019 August
Bolivia South America Half Day Roadtrip

La Paz, the highest capital city in the world. What does it feel like? We knew very little, because we mostly used La Paz as a hop-off point for our trip to other seductive corners of Bolivia, but it wasn’t a wonder that we could relegate to the back-burner for long. So off we went on a short walk around the city to find out.

Before we got to walking around La Paz, we had to get there. And since we arrived here by air, our first introduction to La Paz was the airport, which technically wasn't in La Paz at all. It was in an adjacent city (and higher, as the name suggests), El Alto. It was a cold rainy day when we landed but thankfully for us, it was a warm welcome into the country, much like our experience with the Bolivian Consulate back in Chile.
Cliched as it might seem, traffic just happens to be the first thing one notices in this city. The arterial road that drove out from La Paz into El Alto and then beyond was almost always a mess with vehicles veering in and out constantly, after which they seemed to climb up or descend down into the numerous small roads.
Every once in a while, one could spot really old buses here. They swiftly turned corners and suddenly appeared, as if from a time machine. Microbuses as they were called, were commonly found here in the cities and connected the centre with the various suburbs. Microbuses weren't the only form of transport though, there were the smaller (and more dangerous) mini buses, taxis and trufis that filled up the remaining vacant spaces on the road too.
I was curious about the other famed transport in La Paz, which was the Teleferico, which was considered the longest cable-car system in the world. Well, thanks to its existence, moving around La Paz was far more convenient; that is if we were local. We went on one of the circuits purely for checking out the city from other perspectives ...
... such as this one. I noted that sometimes entire areas had a cluster of buildings that had unplastered, brick walls. Whether it was by accident, regulatory, driven by practicality or a mere reflection of the various strata of society in La Paz, I was not sure. But when I started noticing it, it was noticed across the cityscape.
A ride on the Teleferico was interesting but a visit to Mirador Killi Killi, was far more interesting. From here, La Paz looked its complete self, and imposing too. It almost seemed representative of its entire class, that is of overcrowded and ever expanding cities with no exit plan or end in sight. But at the same time, it was a marvel, yet another human endeavour. Only the snow-capped mountains gave away the fact that La Paz itself was way above sea-level.
We were soon eager to descend to the streets below that belonged to the common folk; the citizens, street-vendors, office-goers and schoolchildren.
Sometimes the streets also had Mariachi Bands out of nowhere. We found this group occupying itself with its music while the entire city moved on unfazed on the footpath as well as in the street.
Across the street adjacent to the Witches Market, we roamed the market to find another kind of music, rather musical instrument. This one was called a Pan Flute. Unfortunately I did not have the time to engage in any kind of cultural activity of Bolivia. Neither did I have the inclination to stuff my backpack with instruments I did not know to play.
The market was full of other touristy items too, especially garments made out of Alpaca wool which happened to be a big hit with many of the tourists. Having said that, most of La Paz wasn't touristy at all because it simply minded its own business as the tourists such as us transit in and out and move about in some of its streets.
When darkness descended on the city, we found the way back to our hostel. La Paz was now full of men and women returning from work or the last of the folks making it downhill from El Alto. As we sat back and reminisced about the day's events we noticed a woman, for whom it was just another evening where the churn ended and before going home, she just needed to buy that one last thing.
