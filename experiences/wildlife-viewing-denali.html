Wildlife Viewing in Denali National Park
Wildlife Viewing in Denali National Park

Timeline : 2018 August
United States of America Alaska North America Full Day Wildlife Nature

We got onto the transit bus to Wonder Lake, one of the farthest campgrounds inside the Denali National park. Springtime being good tourist season, the bus was full and we managed to occupy the last second row. With my camera ready, I glued my eyes to the window of the bus, carefully scanning the vast landscape for any movement

It is not everyday that the weather is so perfect that a regular transit bus trip compares with a game drive through the National Park.
Our bus driver Kat slowed down as she had spotted some tiny little white spots far away, precariously perched on the mountain slopes: Dall's Sheep, a native of Denali national park, rarely seen.
A brown (grizzly) bear was wandering on the slopes of the mountain, looking like a small ball of fur with two little springers (four to six month old cubs usually born in winter and venture outside for the first time during spring, hence the name)
Our next stop was for a pair of caribou, a bull and a female, spotted a little away from our bus. The females have smaller antlers that they do not shed their antlers yearly.
The males sport red antlers just before the rut, when they finally shed their antlers before growing them fresh each year.
The landscape was enormous. Huge mountains, vast valleys extending as far as the eyes can see, grey streams of glacial water, converging and branching out, as though like strands of hair braided.
The transit system, the park rules, even the backcountry camping restrictions and permits are all designed keeping the best interest of the wildlife as a priority. The bus drivers were patient, sensitive to wildlife and were trained in spotting wildlife and knew about their behaviour.
We reached the Eisleson Centre, where we could walk around a little exploring the various view points and hiking trails. A marmot hopped around, curiously looking at the visitors, keeping a safe distance.
I got some time to sit back and observe this creature as it went about foraging for crumbs.
Around noon, the bus reached its final destination, the Wonder Lake, which is also a campground. The calm blue waters reflecting the mountains is a perfect location to spend some time, watch wildlife or just contemplate.
A caribou in the waters caught my attention. Soon it was time to get back to the bus for our return journey. For some of us this was just another stop on our journey, for a few lucky ones who booked their spots in advance, it was a beautiful camping site.
Just as I was thinking that we had seen everything we could, there was excitement in the bus as a large brown bear decide to walk right in front of our bus on the road. The driver requested all of us to be extremely silent as we watched it cross the road.
The weather was warm and sunny, maximizing the wildlife activity around the park. I spotted a large moose down below in the valley, a little distance from the road. On closer look through the binoculars, I could also see a man trying to photograph it at close range. For a few seconds, both the moose and the man were still, probably startled by eachother.
The magnificent mountains were providing the perfect backdrop for our photographs. The elusive Denali peak, the highlight of the park was clearly visible for the entire duration of our trip.
A brown (grizzly) bear was wandering on the slopes of the mountain, looking like a small ball of fur, although enormous in size, it was barely visible against the vast rugged landscape. Through the binoculars, I could clearly see its muzzle, its warm brown eyes, a wild version of the cuddly toy 'teddy' bear.
Across the landscape, in the valley, we could spot three backpackers hiking along the river bed. A grizzly was walking towards them, precariously close. From the height, we could see what the hikers could not due to the dense growth. It was a tense moment, with everyone in the bus anxious for the hikers, who were unaware of what they would encounter.
We were on a different bus after our lunch stop at Eisleson Centre. New fellow travellers, who probably were not as tired as I was, helped raise my enthusiasm by signalling the bus to stop several times more for some more caribou, moose and bear sightings.
As we reached closer to our final destination, the Savage river campground, I could spot a great horned owl perched on one of the branches. The journey was not over yet!
And, just when I was thinking we were finally done for the day, I could spot a large moose in the bushes. She had a calf with her, motionless, as she stood, waiting for the right opportunity to cross the road.
