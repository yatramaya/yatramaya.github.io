Moving With the Mail

Timeline : 2018 February
Uganda Africa Full Day Roadtrip

I had read about the post bus: a bus service run by the postal department of Uganda. However, nothing that I had read would prepare us for what we were about to go through. At 4:30am, lugging our heavy backpacks, we undertook a short, yet scary walk through the roads of Kampala to reach the post office. In most parts of Uganda, especially in busy market areas of down-town Kampala, where we stayed, near the bus stand, we were afraid of getting mugged while walking in the darkness. Although we had walked these streets the previous night, when it was crowded with people walking, vehicles stuck in traffic and numerous shops and delis that dotted these busy streets, the whole place now seemed dark and deserted, with a few street lights to give us comfort. After about fifteen minutes, we reached the well-lit post office which seemed to already be hosting about 6 to 7 people who were planning to travel in the post bus that morning. Here, after waiting for about two hours, and after numerous protests by irate co-passengers, we were ushered into, to our horror, Matatus that were hired to take us to the bus. The frightful, lonely walk from our hotel to the post office started feeling like a joke as we boarded a crowded Matatu at day break. To my dismay, and utter shock, the Matatu took the same roads that we had painstakingly trekked this morning to take us right next to our hotel into the main bus stand! A walk that we could have easily undertaken in two minutes after getting two extra hours of sleep!

All the advantage I had thought we got by turning up early to the post office was lost when we had to get into an almost full bus, where we were made to feel grateful for the two seats that we could manage to squeeze into, along with our backpacks. And if I had wrongly assumed that everything henceforth would be smoother, then quite obviously I had assumed wrong. The crowded bus spent close to two hour waiting right at the bus stand leaving me feeling miserable. It was almost four hours since we had departed from our room and yet we found ourselves at exactly the same place, just two minutes away from our hotel. To make this experience of waiting worse, there were numerous hawkers getting into the bus every two minutes to sell us random stuff. It felt as though we were intended to stay put there to provide them with a captive audience for their sales pitch. Already four hours behind schedule, and with no assured time of reaching our destination, my hopes of getting a decent and safe accommodation started to vanish. The bus ride itself was far from comfortable. With upright, old, rickety seats, that threatened to fall apart any moment, the old, red bus was sluggish, noisy and bumpy, spewing dark smoke each time it was made to climb an incline. I was afraid it might just breakdown on the way, leaving us stranded in the middle of nowhere.

After several hours of what felt like a lifetime, we were approaching our destination, Kabale, a town we knew nothing about. The fact that we had to find an accommodation and also transport the very next day to reach Bwindi was weighing heavily on my mind as I got off the bus with certain relief of having survived the harrowing journey. It was already sun down. We did the act of purposefully walking towards the nearest decent hotel to keep away touts and make them feel like we knew where we were going. All I was looking forward after this long, sweaty, back breaking bus journey was a decent room, a shower that works and chilled beer. We were thankful that we got all of this and some free entertainment of having a rare chance of watching Hindi soap operas on Zee TV, an Indian channel, dubbed in English! What was more amusing to me was to see the hotel staff of “Capricorn Executive Hotel”, completely involved in the overly melodramatic TV serial, cheering for the characters and shedding a tear or two for the characters!
