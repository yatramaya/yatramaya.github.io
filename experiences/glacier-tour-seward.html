Glacier Tour, Seward, Alaska
Glacier Tour, Seward, Alaska

Timeline : 2018 August
United States of America Alaska North America Half Day Wildlife Nature Boat

A bald eagle swooped in very close to our boat as we set off. The sun was struggling to shine through the dense grey clouds that threatened to pour down anytime. To be sailing off into the icy cold waters of the Pacific, with limited visibility, in the hope of seeing some glaciers, started to seem a bit ambitious. The misty cold morning confined most of us to the cozy interior of the boat, with little to look forward to other than this bald eagle and a friendly otter that did not seem to mind either our boat or the ice cold water. We were at the southern tip of the beautiful piece of land whose geography was aligned with Canada, yet politically, it belonged to the rather far United Stated: Alaska. Seward, a commercial fishing town situated on the Resurrection Bay, is famous for its annual Salmon run. It developed into a tourist hotspot for most of the cruises, short and long ones, that originated from this nondescript town, owing to its proximity to the glaciers coupled with the convenience of a railroad terminal and good road connectivity to the capital.

We were the lucky few who had managed to get hold of a tour on one of the last days for the season, before the weather would make it non-feasible. So not only we had already taken our chances by choosing the tail-end of the season but also, unpredictable weather was threatening to washout our dream of seeing a glacier. I consoled myself watching the enthusiastic kayakers who were undeterred by the weather and were ready to jump into the water at Fox Island, which was our first stop. After dropping off three kayakers and wishing them luck, our boat was ready to venture into the open sea. It started to drizzle just as I caught sight of bright orange beaks of puffins that were bobbing their heads in and out of the water near Fox Island. My hopes started to soar as I started seeing more wildlife. A large group of Stellar Sea Lions found the weather perfect to sun themselves on the shore of some of the smaller islands we passed by. A pair of Dall’s porpoises’ tiny spouts were spotted in the water, soon followed by a view of their fins. These were much smaller than dolphins.

From here, as we navigated the rough waters, I looked forward to the most exciting part of this cruise - the vast glaciers of the NW fjords. From a distance I could see the icy blue mountains starting to appear on the side of our boat, soon growing in size so as to engulf us from three sides. It was the first time that I had seen a glacier up close. Nothing that I had imagined ever came close to the experience of actually seeing one - up to 70 feet of snow, compacted, over several thousands of years, giving them the cool blue colour. The entire mountain felt like it was glowing with this faint blue light. I understood that these glaciers were receding. Many that existed here earlier, did not any more. As our boat got closer, I could see huge blocks of ice floating in the water, occupied by seals that were silently drifting on these icy islands.

Once we approached close enough, the engines were cut and there was a deathly silence. For sometime, the only thing I could hear was the cracking sounds that would occur along with the breaking off of huge chunks of ice that crashed into the water. At certain places, a flow of icy water would erupt, like a waterfall, leading sometimes to a crack in the ice, and eventually the break-off. I thought I could watch this phenomena forever. It seemed like one of the most fascinating action sequences of nature, yet happening continuously, slowly and silently. I started looking out for thin streams of water hoping to catch the breaking of the ice. I experienced the mixed feeling of excitement of seeing such a phenomenon coupled with the sadness of recording the frequency with which it was happening, reminding me that at this rate, these mountains would vanish sooner. Sometimes I would be looking in some direction, and the crashing sound would be heard elsewhere, only to quickly turn around to catch the end of falling stream of the ice into the cold water. The calls of seagulls heightened the effect of this deathly sequence.

The few minutes that we spent, watching these unbelievable sights and sounds, sometimes trying to photograph it, felt like hours, with time stretching to eternity and etching this in my memory forever. We hadn’t realized that the weather had gotten worse, with a storm threatening to break out any moment. It was soon time to turn around and leave the company of these towering blocks of ice with a hope that they would somehow continue to exist forever. On of our way back, our captain announced that she could spot some whale activity which made all of us excited and run to the deck braving the rain. It must have been a grey whale. I got a glimpse the of the tail fin as it splashed into the water.

Then our boat meandered through some dreamy landscapes; mounds of green islands floating in water, in the mist. It was also time to spot some additional wildlife up close as our boat passed these islands. I could identify some: tufted puffins, common Murre and sea lions. The boat slowed down as it navigated the narrow pathways between the landmasses that were jutting out, giving us an excellent photo opportunity with the birds. Despite the gloomy weather and the rough waters, I was satisfied with the sightings and the unparalleled experience of standing in front of a glacier and feeling its enormity, something that up until then was all theory for me.
