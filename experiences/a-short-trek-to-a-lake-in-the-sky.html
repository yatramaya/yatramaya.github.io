A Short Trek to a Lake in the Sky

Timeline : 2017 October
India Asia Multi Day Mountain Historical Culture

Our host, over evening cup of hot tea mentioned about the Dhankar lake that was a must visit. I was happy that there were not many guests either at our homestay or any other around, which meant that there would be lesser crowds at the lake the next morning. Our host offered to pack us Aloo Paranthas to carry on our little expedition. We started off on a trail from Dhankar Gompa that would take us further higher by another 200-300 metres, to this high altitude lake at 4150 meters. The sun was barely up and was not yet felt. It was a steep, but short trek with a clearly defined path which we could easily follow on our own. This is, if I could see that path through the cloud that I was pushing out through my mouth. Initially we were on the same trail as a couple who were transporting supplies on the backs of a few donkeys. At some point they took a diversion and crossed over to the next mountain. A community, a village, a home? Even higher than the Gompa? I wondered. My questions were quelled when there emerged a few snow capped mountains into my view. Breathing became a chore as the air became thinner; I had to take frequent breaks. I was glad to have avoided carrying a heavy backpack.

As I walked further up the trail, I could see the path descend a bit, into what could only be the lake. It was not visible at first but started to reveal itself slowly as we walked past some of the rocks. It was turquoise blue, glimmering in the sunlight, absolutely stunning and inviting. After a trek, I was tempted to take a dip in the icy cool waters or at least take off my shoes and dip my feet. But there were clear signs that this was a holy lake where taking dips, swimming and other human activities were not permitted. There was an ancient stupa close to the lake. There was a peaceful silence. There was nobody other than the two of us, a few ducks and other birds. The entire experience was special. The water was so clear that I could see the ground beneath; it was shallow at the edges. This was a lake fed by glaciers and would be frozen for almost eight months in a year. The snow capped mountains surrounding the lake with their reflection in the clear water was out of the world.

Sitting at the shores of this holy lake, I closed my eyes and felt a deep sense of calmness. I could feel the tiredness of the trek melting away. All the weariness of the trip undertaken so far, the biting cold that I felt in my toes, the mild headache due to reduced oxygen; none of those seemed to matter any more. I was humbled by this wonderful creation and just sat there mesmerized by the sheer beauty of the place. It truly felt holy just as many other holy places must feel, like how Manasarovar might feel but without the people, the crowds, the human presence and the inevitable desecration that we bring along. The littering, the chatter, the colourful clothes; everything associated with our presence in such places starts to feel like pollution. It was not so with the ducks and the geese. They blended in perfectly well. With a keen sense that I had felt earlier too in the mountains, I had a desire to blend into the surroundings; merge into its singular whole. Was it a death wish? Was it just my fantasy? I think no. It was a sense of belonging.
