Words cannot describe vibes, Arambol, Goa

Timeline : 2022 November
India Asia Multi Day People Culture Beach

On our very first trip to Goa, the mystical beach of Arambol was but a short two hour visit; an interlude between the many places we visited. Back then, it was sort of an insatiable hunger, perhaps greed even to “see it all”. It was a ride on a ramshackle rented bike from Vagator - where we were shacked up; an evening ride got us there just in time for a spectacular sunset at the Arambol beach. The rocky formations that distinguished this beach from several others that we had visited on that trip, the sunset turning the sky purple with the jagged edges of the blackened rocks in the foreground and the crowds dressed in Gothic with Rastafarian hairstyles walking along the beach stayed with me long after we returned home.

The next time I went to Goa was after more than decade and I knew that we had to go to Arambol to spend more time there. It was just the shoulder season before the peak New Year rush. The crowds had arrived but not in full force; there was enough happening, yet not in a way that would make us feel crowded. The vibe had not changed much and the beach was still pristine. This time, I could soak in more of the place, explore the food and ingest the culture a little more. Our day revolved mostly around multiple visits to the beach, either to swim, to lounge on the sand or to just walk the length of the beach. This is when we discovered a hostel and restaurant, the name which suggested that our quest had ended, both physically and spiritually. “This is it”, it was called. The evening music drew us there and retained us till wee hours of morning. The music was a mix of several genres, integrating cultures from across the world. Musicians converged there to perform together - some rookies included - from various countries. Overall, it was an electric atmosphere, yet very cozy and casual. The food, which was primarily Turkish enticed us with some traditional and well-known specialities, which transported me to Istanbul, even if for a fleeting moment. When, after a few traditional Turkish dishes, we ordered the Baklava for dessert, the chef came to meet us. He probably wanted to know who his patrons were. We generously complimented him for the experience.

The music brought us back the next night as well. Performances ranged from Kannada rap to the revolutionary anthem Bella Ciao that everyone joined in chorus. There were soulful solo performances and impromptu bands that came together on stage. There were some who knew each other from previous years and many who got introduced for the first time. Overall, there was good camaraderie among the performers, which reflected in their flowing music. I felt that Russia was represented more, although there were several countries that came together; music that flowed seamlessly without borders and forgotten differences. It felt so wonderful to be amidst all those passionate musicians that time came to a standstill and the moments were stretched to eternity. This is it. That was that.
