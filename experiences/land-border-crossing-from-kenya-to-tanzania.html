Land Border Crossing from Kenya to Tanzania

Timeline : 2018 March
Kenya Tanzania Africa Half Day Roadtrip

We left Kisii early in the morning, after a breakfast consisting of coffee and Mandazi. The Matatu stand was close to where we stayed and this gave us the advantage of walking right up to it with our backpacks. As expected, someone “fished” us and led us through the maze of Matatus parked to a specific one that said Kisii Direct. We had learned that “direct” means no changes/transfers midway. Then again, we knew enough by now - practical and experienced in this part of the world - that what they promised was not always what they delivered. So we sat in the Matatu with two more fellow passengers waiting for ours to fill up so that we could leave. It took almost an entire hour, during which we had to endure the persistent onslaught of hawkers selling various wares from boiled eggs and banana to electric shavers and power banks. They were relentless. But were friendly enough to smile at us and coax us to buy their products, sometimes knowing very well that we would not need. Selling an electric shaver to my partner with a beard became a joke of sorts among them and they tried to pull it on us multiple times, just for laughs.

Finally the Matatu left Kisii. We were on the road that connected Kisii to Migori, which was known as the sugar-belt since - unsurprisingly - they grew sugarcane here mostly. We saw a few sugar factories as well. The road was under construction and hence the ride was quite a bumpy one. The road snaked through various sugarcane plantations, villages, trees, with magnificent views of the far away hills. I remember this distinctly because up until then, the scenery we had experienced in Kenya was very different. It was overall quite a green part of Kenya that was very different from the Rift valley and Masai areas we had experienced earlier. It was cooler too.

We reached Migori after several stops to pick up and drop off people travelling shorter distances. At Migori, as we had expected, they asked us to get off from the “direct” Matatu and board another one delivered us to Isabenia/Sirare which is the last village on the Kenya-Tanzania border. We had only read a grand total of one person’s account of crossing Isabenia border through road. Although locals used this frequently to cross into Tanzania, it was not well known or documented in the tourist/traveller circuit, compared to the more popular crossing at Namanga on the densely traversed Nairobi-Arusha road. But this did not deter us from trying our luck at the lesser popular border crossing. We knew we would save a lot of distance and reach a much peaceful village in Tanzania, which was going to be our base-camp to the Serengeti park.

Once we walked to the immigration office on the Kenyan side, we were stamped out of Kenya without any issues. So, half the job done, we walked a little more confidently to the Tanzanian immigration office. Unlike some of the border crossings that are one-stop (which means that both the countries will have immigration at the same building), this one was a two-stop. But that fact did not make the process difficult at all as the officers were friendly and guided us to the right places. Once we entered the Sirare immigration office at the Tanzanian side of the border, we had to go through a security check for our luggage, show our yellow fever certificates and put down our contacts in their register. We were on our way to the visa counter. A very friendly officer, who on seeing us (tourists), walked out of his counter, came to us and helped us with the form filling. Then he went back in his seat behind the counter and scanned our passports and completed the process. We were issued a three month Tanzanian visa, which was as simple a stamp on which a number was scribbled. We smiled as we completed the seamless process. All we needed now was a bank. Thankfully, there was one right inside the immigration office, to our relief. Thus, we avoided all the touts and completed some currency conversion safely. Our walk to the bus station on the Tanzanian side must have been a picture of confidence because at this point we were in a position to recommend this crossing to anybody and assure them it is possible to complete without encountering any issues. It was also far less crowded and we were relaxed.
