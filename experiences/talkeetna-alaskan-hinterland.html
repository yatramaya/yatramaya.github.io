Talkeetna : Alaskan Hinterland
Talkeetna : Alaskan Hinterland

Timeline : 2018 August
Alaska United States of America North America Half Day Culture Historical Market River

As I walked the barely asphalted road that led us from the well wooded section that closely guarded our hideout into what was the active and colourful part of the town, I started realizing why I liked this place so much. For one, it was unlike the rest of the United States. There was an authentic small-town feel to it. Not the sterile and templated small-town that one gets used to in the U.S.A but a genuine wild and free feel. I guess one could actually shout out loud and breathe free here, although I did not try it out to prove myself right. Alaska itself felt like another country without the stereotypes and cliches that rest of the mainland offered but Talkeetna, the little town that was our stopover before heading out to Denali offered something more for the soul.

We had chosen a hostel named “Talkeetna Hostel International” which was only a few minutes or so from the central part of the town but was hard to find without a good map thanks to the amount of forested section one had to pass through. The hostel was any idealist backpackers dream with minimal yet comfortable setup, basic tariff and extremely friendly and helpful staff. I knew I’d have a great time as soon as I stepped in through the creaky door into the wooden structure dripping wet in the rain. There were helpful instructions all around and enough consideration for anyone who might walk in wet and needs some warmth. A kitchen for some self cooking, beverages and most importantly some heat and a bath. Most importantly, there were other travellers who were in the desirable business of actually chatting with other human beings instead of staring at their phones.

Our walk was taking us towards the town itself and one could not miss the Talkeetna Airport that was ever visible on the left. We spotted the tiny airplanes that were used for flight-seeing. Flight-seeing was where one was taken on sightseeing trips on these chartered planes and the ones that headed out to Denali were the most popular among tourists. A pricey affair but perhaps well worth the view for someone who did not intend to actually trek or climb up the mountain. Talkeetna offered a lot more for the adventurous; jet-boat tours, cruises along the river, dog sled, zip-line, fishing and what not. Since I had my plans for visiting the Denali National Park the day after, Talkeetna for me was mainly about exploring town and resting.

As the town started to reveal itself I noticed how touristy it actually was once we got off the section where the hostel was located. Small and old structures made of wood screamed about selling something or the other; mostly adventure services but also curios, art and a lot of food and beverages. That being true, it was a relief to not see any of the big chains that are an eyesore in the rest of the country. What I learnt was that many of these businesses have been in Talkeetna for over a hundred years now since the time people started moving in and making for themselves a life around here seeking minerals, resources and gold. Talkeetna has a rich and story-worthy history connected to the history of this region itself with the railroad being an important part of it.

The main street in Talkeetna was looking like a fair with tourists walking up and down and taking in the views and busy staff in the various establishments struggling to manage the crowds, all the while maintaining a cheerful demeanour. We decided to walk up the main street all the way to the end for a view of the Susitna River, actually the confluence of Susitna, Chulitna and the Talkeetna rivers. It only takes about ten minutes of walking to get to the Riverfront park from where the views of the river are breathtaking. It would have been even more so if the visibility were good because then I would be taking in some spectacular views of the Denali itself but unluckily for us, it was a cloudy and hazy day.

The unbridled wilderness around the town was inviting and one was always tempted to take a deviation to the left or the right from the main streets of Talkeetna. Where did that little path lead to? Does that little wooden signboard read “trail”? My mind was a mixed bag. I should probably have signed up for a few more days in this town, I thought frequently. I heard that Aurora Dora, the single person establishment famous for photographs of the Aurora ran out of Talkeetna. I had no intention of visiting though. My own aspirations of somehow witnessing an Aurora while in Alaska were strong enough to get me quickly into the Denali National Park where I intended to spend most of the time so as to maximize the chances. A conversation that we had earlier with another traveller who was on her return journey from Denali National Park had already raised my hopes.

All that walking had got me hungry and thirsty. There was much to offer in terms of food but what stood out for us was the Denali Brew Pub which seemed to have on offer a lot of local flavours. So off we went. The place was crowded and loud yet warm and welcoming. We tried the staple items, i.e. burgers, fries and beer which did not fail to please. As we looked out into the street we saw a dog on a leash playfully attack something tiny. While we discussed about Stubbs the cat-mayor of Talkeetna who was mauled by a dog and died of the complications, I noticed that in this case it was only a bird that had landed on the side-walk.

Right across the street was Nagley’s store which we heard was setup in the early 1900s and has been going at it ever since. Interestingly it was also the mayoral office when Stubbs was serving his community. We had the practical need to visit the place since our plan was to tent inside the Denali National Park; some bread, snacks and ready to eat items were in order for the cold and wet days that seemed to be waiting for us. So with that intent we paid up our dues at the Pub and went shopping into the historical convenience store. Once we had what we needed to survive we headed out into the woods where warm beds awaited us. As we walked back, I soaked up everything that Talkeetna offered simultaneously regretting the lack of time in this quaint little town and cherishing the chunky amounts of happiness and warmth it offered in such a short span of time.
