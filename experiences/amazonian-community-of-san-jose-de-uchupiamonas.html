The Amazonian Community of San Jose de Uchupiamonas

Timeline : 2019 August
Bolivia South America Half Day People Wildlife Culture

Very soon, the driver of our car sounded excited and stopped the car by the side of the road. Soon, we were all looking in the direction where he was looking. He had spotted some furry animals; extremely cute rodents. 5 or 6 young ones of the Capybara. The mother was nearby, quite large in comparison. Soon she and her flock disappeared into a burrow. This was our first sighting of Capybaras and we were extremely happy to see them from so near, just by the side of the road. It was a lucky start to what was actually a bird watching tour in the Bolivian Amazon cloud forests of Madidi National Park. We had booked this tour with the Sadiri lodge which was quite a distance away by road from the small town of Rurrenabaque.

This road journey from Rurrenabaque to Sadiri, through the Madidi National Park, was itself a great chance to see the beautiful landscape. Numerous streams crossed our path and fortunately, they were shallow enough so that our car managed to easily wade through. At Tumupasa, our guide, Ricardo bought some fresh Njaranja (Oranges) and Bananas. It was already lunchtime but lunch was at least two hours away at our final destination for the day at the San Jose de Uchupiamonas community. We would briefly visit our lodge to just deposit our backpacks and carry a smaller pack for the three day trek through the jungle. The fruits were welcome and we devoured it along with some other snacks we had packed, which we shared with the crew. We were six in the car, the two of us, Ricardo, our guide, his wife Maria, who was responsible for feeding us, both of whom were from the community of San Jose de Uchupiamonas, Jorge the driver, and Gera, the staff from Sadiri who was kind enough to accompany us to bridge any communication gaps that the language might cause, she was greatly instrumental in putting together an exciting itinerary tailor made for us.

Even while we were eating, Ricardo’s eyes and ears were always tuned to birds and he would keep telling us the names of birds he was spotting. And he would never tire of watching birds and identifying them. Although I was grateful for this genuine interest he showed, I have to admit that I was too tired to match his enthusiasm. But his being that way actually helped my cause; ensuring that I put my best on the table too, so as to have a fruitful birdwatching tour. Even before we entered the forests, he had shown us several birds. This, to a great extent kept our hunger pangs at bay and made the otherwise road trip exciting.

Around 3pm, we reached the remote village of the community of San Jose de Uchupiamonas It was a very rustic, relaxed place with lots of fruiting trees and birds around. We were hosted for lunch at Senora Nelli’s house where we were offered freshly squeezed juice and a sumptuous lunch consisting of Banana flower soup, rice, pulses, salad and a dessert. Everything was freshly cooked with local ingredients, simple, yet flavourful and they tried their best to offer the Amazonian experience to us, even with our dietary constraints, which they were prepared to cater to. We were famished and gleefully devoured the tasty food that was on offer. Although it was tempting to just stay back here and rest for the day, our trip had just begun and our campsite that Gera had chosen for us for the night halt was another two hours and a boat-ride away. Thanking our kind hosts, we drove for another short distance till the river, from where, we would begin our Amazonian adventure.

The next morning, we returned from the campsite and were back at San Jose De Uchupiamonas around 11am and were offered a tour of the village by Ricardo. He took us around the community including into a few homes. While he explained some local processes like de-husking paddy manually with a pounding staff, we pointed out to him that this is exactly what we did in Indian villages back home. We could exchange our local knowledge with theirs and surprise them with how similar some of these were! It was also evident that most of the tourists who came to the village came from the first-world countries such as the U.S, U.K; countries that had completely lost touch with nature, tradition and past which meant the visitors gawked a bit more when certain traditional systems were on display. It was a bit of learning for Ricardo himself that in the eastern cultures such as India, the traditional systems still existed side by side along with what we consider an overrun of the modern.

We visited the local school, where we got a brief on how it was run, what were the issues and political influences that caused problems here. The life of the people here is hard; they live in the middle of the forest, with the nearest town two hours away. The river is one of the most important transport systems. We interacted briefly with the children, one of whom was making friendship bands with wild seeds found in the surroundings. Apart from what the tourism has offered them - bringing in better quality homes and a few more facilities and exposure - their main way of living is by subsistence farming; growing some fruits, vegetables, cocoa, and also poultry and fishing.

We got some insight into the dynamics of how this community was formed and how it was sustained by the tourism due to Chalalan and Sadiri. The founder of Sadiri, Ruth Alipaz, who is from the same community who got a chance to go out and become an entrepreneur returned to setup this lodge as a means of sustainable tourism employing locals for more than 90% of the work and ensuring the money returns to the community. Everyone we met spoke highly of this intervention and the skills that they have been taught part of the training. Ricardo was chosen to get in depth training in bird watching which meets international standards. Some of the women like Nelli and Maria were trained as chefs who can prepare various cuisines. A few others in work like construction, repairs etc. The objective of all this was to discourage loggers who are pillaging the forests illegally and poachers who are destroying the biodiversity, by instead promoting eco-tourism which is community driven and sustainable.

These meet-ups, even if in short-bursts, with this small, local and rooted community was a huge learning experience for us. Before we parted, we had a shower and had one last meal at Nelli’s home; a sumptuous lunch consisting of vegetable soup, pineapple juice, boiled yucca, mani curry (peanut sauce with yucca), boiled rice and to top it, a cooked banana dessert. We were offered hammocks to take our afternoon siesta into which we immediately fell asleep. After a short nap, we were ready for our walk to our next campsite.
