Whale Watching at Mirissa, Sri Lanka
Whale Watching at Mirissa, Sri Lanka

Timeline : 2018 January
Sri Lanka Asia Half Day Wildlife Boat

This was nothing close to what I had imagined. The boat was rather small. The waves were menacingly rough, and threatened to throw the boat off balance at the slightest opportunity. There were about 20 people on the boat, most of them in various stages of sea sickness. The lucky ones, like myself, who were miraculously unaffected by the rough sea, were on the deck with our eyes glued to the waters. My ears were cautiously listening to our captain for any sightings. He, on the other hand, was enjoying this ride on the bumpy waters. Being a native and having the sea for company since childhood, he was clearly in his elements. I had to hold on firmly to the railings of the boat to avoid getting tossed overboard.

Whale watching in this part of the world is not always enjoyable. I had read horror stories of terrible captains and crew members who were abusive and who did not care for the passengers. Moreover, what terrified me was stories about how they would chase the whales into coves, harass them AND go extremely close to them. Whales are very shy, gentle creatures and can get extremely stressed with such interference, especially if they have calves swimming along. I had spent most of my research time trying to find a boat that would not do any of this. And, I was lucky to come across this too-good-to-be-true company, run by a spirited bunch of young men, that did not carry any review less than five stars. And I could see why. The crew was extremely helpful to all passengers, treated everyone with respect and kept the humour going as it can be very difficult to manage a bunch of sea-sick strangers on uncharted territory. The captain, who was also the owner of the boat, was very careful to not disturb the animals. He would drive to places where he felt they would be present, without chasing them, without going too close, allowing them to come nearby and explore us, giving them time to go where they wanted, and graciously following them at a distance. And, I could see that he truly enjoyed the sea and its creatures.

There it was. The unmistakable spout. A sure giveaway to the presence of this beautiful creature. We counted till 10 and then, she came out. I spotted a glimpse of her fin and then she was gone. I had travelled to the southern- most edge of Sri Lanka and ventured into the Indian Ocean to see one of the most marvellous creatures on the planet. An adult blue whale is about 25 metres in length; longer than our boat! The next few hours were spent hearing “oohs” and “aaahs” from the audience. We were lucky to get several sightings. One of them was extremely close to our boat. I could see the entire animal; almost in its full length emerged out of the water and disappeared alongside our boat. Of course, none of this resulted in great photographs as I had realized that watching a whale and taking a picture, with the right timing is close to impossible, for me at the least. We can never be sure where it would pop out next. And the excitement did not allow me to stay calm enough to keep my hands steady.

It was as though I was under a spell. Everything about this trip felt surreal - the boat, the rough sea and the whales swimming around. Only when our captain called out that it was way past noon and the sea was about to get rougher that this spell was broken and I woke up to reality. It was already six hours since we started. Even with all that tossing around, I was not exhausted. In fact, I felt it was over too soon. I would have liked to us to follow these gentle giants for some more time.
