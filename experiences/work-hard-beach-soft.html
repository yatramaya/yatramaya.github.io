Work Hard Beach Soft

Timeline : 2013 February
United States of America North America Half Day Beach

As we drove farther to the west, the landscape changed from the usual staple of large buildings with their even-larger parking lots, to one, that in appearance, had a little more of what I conjure up in my mind whenever I come across the word ‘California’. A friend of mine had once told me I’d fit right in because I was a tree-hugger. In the literal sense of that term, at least, this seemed the most likely possibility as I witnessed here some of the largest, tallest and oldest redwood trees; trees whose circumference would have made me look like an insect hanging on to the bark when attempting to hug one. Our spontaneous drive today took us through a stretch of such trees, and, in preparation, had taken much lesser time and effort than buying the packet of chips we were carrying for the journey.

About 30 minutes ago we were at our workplace in a locality within the Bay Area called Palo Alto. Memories of my workplace are always associated with the dense, air-conditioned and sound-proofed interiors of an office but on stepping outside, being greeted with a cold breeze that was punctuated liberally with the whizzing past of countless auto-mobiles just a few meters away on US-101. The free-way felt like a portal that took me to office everyday and back obscuring all else around; especially the fact that there was a large water body to the east and a mountain range to the west that descended into the Pacific Ocean. On one of the days, post my office hours, a decision was made to drive to the western edges of this land, tracing the contour lines of the North American continent. Perhaps it was the practicality or even the casual nature of it that makes it so endearing today when I think about it. Or perhaps it is the sense of loss and missed opportunities because we did it only once.

Our destination on that day was the Half Moon Bay, a small beach town that was best visited during the summers. Seasonal privileges were something not available to us, ultra-short-term visitors. As it was the month of February, it was still a mix of bitter winter cold that was threatening to overstay this year and an inviting Sun with blue skies that tempted one with promises of warmer days to come. The weather was just perfect on the day we chose for this trip. We stopped over for a quick snack at a hole-in-the-wall eatery and with the practised and conditioned dependency on the navigation system in our car proceeded to our destination. The businesses and busy intersections were soon left behind and we could see a lot of individual homes with a car, a truck or a trailer parked adjacent to them. I noticed that we were obviously ascending, albeit gently. Soon even the homes and smaller buildings diminished in number and it was clear that the roads were passing through protected, forested areas. Traffic on the road was now restricted to a very few cars, but I considered the probability of crowded roads, had we chosen a summer weekend to drive to this beach, which was only half an hour away from most of the Bay Area.

Half Moon Bay was known so for its shape, if one shared the imaginative eyes of whoever named it so. As we arrived, the Sun was preparing to sign off not just into the West but also far beyond what was the agreed timelines for an official new day in the Eastern countries. However there was still plenty of sunlight. I parked the car in the nearest slot available which was incidentally a picturesque location in itself, as if the car had just abruptly stopped at the edge of a cliff. A table and bench nearby was quickly claimed by us for a short yet significant picnic that was in race against the sun. A few feet below, the beach was getting rid of its last few resilient visitors who were braving the cold and making a last ditch effort to take in everything a beach had to offer; well, everything except trying to get into the water; the water must have been too cold for that season and that day. Some people were walking dogs, who, quite evidently, did not understand their master’s urgency in hurrying back to the parked cars. It is essentially incomplete if beaches do not have children losing themselves in the sand and the sea or running behind their belongings that were being snatched away by the elements but it was what it was; a boring evening with just a few adults who had made it there on a busy working day. Looking far ahead into the horizon I could spot a few lights belonging to floats, ships or vessels of various sizes, all of them having something to do with the larger city of San Francisco that was far north on the same coastline.

Eventually, and with complete disregard to any kinds of hope against hope anyone might have, the beach was covered in darkness. The cliff-like patch of land, where the car was parked was now a dark abyss with only the sound of waves from the Pacific to give us any indication of a beach beyond. The moment of return was here. It felt odd to realize the opposite; that yet again that it would take just half an hour for returning to our daily lives; this turned out like a visit to a movie theatre. In a weird way, this short picnic did share that one trait with movies; it felt terribly unreal back then, and it feels the same years after.
