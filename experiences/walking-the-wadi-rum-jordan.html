Walking the Wadi Rum, Jordan
Walking the Wadi Rum, Jordan

Timeline : 2018 April
Jordan Asia Multi Day Historical Nature Trekking

How far would one have to drive out to actually get somewhere? Strange were my thoughts as I stared out of the SUV into the emptiness all around. Did we really have to get anywhere at all? In a desert like Wadi Rum it took only an hour worth of driving to get to a point where one’s source and destination started to look the same and considering we did not have any specific goal it was only logical to rely on our host who was intently driving onwards on a trail that could not have been older than a week. I could see the dust cloud raised from our own vehicle had already gotten to the task of covering the tire track we would have left behind.

When I looked out of the rear window it became clear why this place had a magnetic effect on tourists. The settlement where we had met our host a few minutes earlier was slowly fading away in the background, diffused by the sand below and almost eaten up by the mountains on either side that simply jutted out of a large bed of sand. It wasn’t a wonder that while the movie “Lawrence of Arabia” introduced this place to most of the world, recent movies such as “The Martian” chose it as a location for its depiction of the red planet. If I ignored the earthly groceries that I could spot in the boot at the peripheries of my vision, I might as well have been on a trip to the Martian surface.

We had signed up for a two days and two nights in the desert with tenting and driving around and hopefully avoiding the many other tourists and camps that were scattered around the Wadi. Our first little excursion today was in fact by foot, near the settlement itself (known as the Wadi Rum Village) while we waited for our host to sort out the plans for the next two days. Around half a kilometre from the village and quite visible from there is the Nabatean Temple, or to put it precisely the ruins of a Nabatean Temple. Nabateans are an ancient Arabic Tribe who settled down in Jordan, Syria and even Saudi Arabia and Palestine. At a time when their empire flourished, Petra was their capital. The temple was not much to look at and although it is considered that it was colourful, today its ruins blend into the reddish orange backdrop. We spent a few minutes at the location, almost directionless as we did not have the necessary guides to understand the significance or history of the place.

When we returned to the village, the SUV that was to take us around for the next few days was loaded and ready. We were asked to load our personal belongings including the tents and then we were off. And here we were now exiting what seemed to be a large corridor, the other end of which was now completely invisible. The corridor opened up to a sea of sand but that is not where we were headed to. This we got to know because our host turned a right and then seemed to be driving towards a lone tree that stood at the foothill of another mountain. With no other vegetation around, the shade of the tree seemed like a camp for many others, mostly other guides with their vehicles parked nearby. A lone camel herder was hanging around too with the camels filling in the gaps between the parked vehicles.

Our guide pointed in the direction of the crumbly and rock-strewn mountain nearby and asked us to hike independently. He assured us that some tea would be ready on our return. Originally and locally known as “Ain Abu Aineh” and today known as “Lawrence’s Spring” it is the only water source for miles around and has historically served caravans as it serves camel groups today. A short trek up the rubble and rock takes one to the source of the spring which cannot be missed due to the only green spots that line its flow all the way to the base. Since our trip was in summer, the spring had dried down to a thin stream. Not feeling brave enough to actually drink the water I walked further north instead towards a rock that could be used as a resting point.

The views were spectacular. And considering these were the first aerial views of Wadi Rum we were getting, I was excited. Down below, the camels were still parked and their guardians were sitting around steaming kettles of tea and chatting away. Far ahead in the distance, someone was already leading a caravan of camels out into the nothingness. Closer below at the foothills, coloured dots decorated the otherwise uniform cream, brown and maroon. Some dots were moving faster than the others. Tourists like us climbing up to the spring. Looking up at the sky, we found the Sun slowly preparing itself for baking us all in this large oven and considering that we had started early to avoid this experience we hurried back down towards our guide. The idea of hot tea even if under the shade of a tree was not very enticing. But if the Bedouin have been doing it for long there must be something to it.

Language was a big barrier. We did not speak even a little Arabic other than the word “Shukhran” which I profusely used when the tea was handed over to me. Thankfully I could converse with our guide in some English while the other guides and herders spoke only Arabic with each other and our guide. Only basic pleasantries were exchanged and before I could gulp down the extremely sweet tea, our guide had already started packing the wares and hinting that we move on. It was our fault really. As per the norms around here, we were offered the usual fare of hanging out in a desert camp somewhere, listen to some songs and perhaps watch a dance performance while enjoying the Bedouin culinary experience. But we had insisted on what we called “the real thing”. We had asked for hikes through the Wadi and tenting and cooking out in the open. Now our guide was basically hinting that chilling under the shade goes against this premise. So we gladly complied.

As we drove out of this little green corner I could see that the landscape opened up. There were trails everywhere and in all directions; both tire tracks as well as camel tracks. But nothing at all in sight. Up ahead I saw copious amounts of sand. It was a sand dune and we were driving towards it. Reddish in colour and sending out waves of heat that could be felt by anyone who sensed that something was up, especially since our guide slowed down and found a nice shady spot under the natural caves and cuts of a nearby mountain facing the dune. In about five minutes we were out there hiking up the dune while our guide took a short nap. The plan was to walk up the dune and enjoy the process as well as the scenery from the summit.

Anyone who has walked on sand knows how tough it can be. And it got only tougher on an incline. Add to that the soaring temperatures and I was tired even before starting. Yet, this was an extremely invigorating experience. Out came our scarves and headgear to protect ourselves from the harsh sun and also sunglasses as by now both the heat and the light were getting to us. Thankfully there wasn’t any harsh winds or sand storms. It was a quiet afternoon in that sense and we were already getting used to the drill. Far ahead I could see another SUV drive past in the valley, like a little toy car. It is only sights like those that reminded me of the size of the Wadi itself which can easily be misjudged from the photographs we were clicking.

The descent from the dune was quick with playful slides and quick bouts of running and yet again some tea waited for us. We chatted up a bit about tea, sugar and trade with our guide who was the only one among us who could lay claim to be a descendant of some of the greatest history influencing traders of all time. The Levant as it is known lies right in the centre of Africa, Europe and Asia and has seen not just trade routes that influenced empires and changed history but also has been the passing route of waves of human races who came out of the African continent to spread to the rest of the planet. There was much to talk about if we talked about the history of this place and how tea and sugar managed to reach the desert was only the beginning of such a story.

We drove to a spot next which we found was named “Alameleh Inscriptions”. There were no signboards or information here apart from the fact that our guide briefly explained to us that the petroglyphs were the creation of the Nabateans or even people before them. The inscriptions looked old enough and etched into many sections of the flat surfaces that composed the mammoth mountain that we were looking at. It is not that these inscriptions were at any particular location. They could be found scattered all over Wadi Rum and even beyond. Our minimal understanding of the matter and inability to interpret or understand them were the only reason why we moved on after a few photographs. We were however left impressed by the human effort itself. Especially our ability to tell stories because that is what these inscriptions were. Stories of the lives of people who passed through and lived here thousands of years ago.

Our guide meanwhile was scouting for a good spot for lunch and turning the corner, I found him nearby setting up the stove already. Lunch was a simple affair over a high-pressure cooking fuel driven stove, some traditional bread that was re-heated and canned beans with tomato sauce. Probably it would have been a more elaborate affair if we weren’t vegans. Our seemingly weird dietary choice had left our guide confused and he had decided at the beginning of the journey that it would be best to take us to a grocery store and buy whatever we could eat. That made it simple, as we could identify canned food and bread without much effort. Tired as we were, we gorged on the stuff and welcomed the suggestion of a nap. The short nap lasted for about 3 hours and this phenomenon can only be understood and appreciated by someone who has indulged in short naps on an extremely hot day under a rapid ceiling fan. When we woke up, it was yet again time for another tea, the third one during our trip. And off we were sent on yet another hike.

This time our guide decided to unhinge us a bit more and gave us theoretical instructions based on natural formations in the far distance. Unused though we were to such instructions, we managed to grasp the simplicity of it. I mean, one has to be really unlucky to miss a simple instruction such as walk towards “that rock jutting out” and then take a right and then walk towards “that mountain”. There weren’t that many things jutting out and then there weren’t people or noises or other distractions. So we took off on a long walk to experience the desert like conditions. This time the ground was more of gravel and thorny shrubs and this was a novelty. The walk was long and arduous and at one point the distance from where we started from and “that rock jutting out” started to look exactly the same and exactly how it was when we began. Nevertheless, after pushing ourselves we were at the end of the walk and had experienced quite a bit of the harshness that the desert offered. Thankfully we were walking on the eastern side of a mountain and the setting sun meant that the eastern sides were much cooler. One only has to step out from sun into the shades of the mountains to witness the difference in temperature that was on offer. We were met at the end by our smiling guide who had driven there via another route and setup camp for the night. “That is it for today” he declared.

Evening was spent setting up our tent and some short walks and photography. The place turned into a beautiful orange, red and blue and presented us with views that were out of this world. Our guide was busy preparing dinner though. Since lunch was a makeshift affair he had decided for dinner to be slightly better. So a proper fire was setup with collected wood and a few more items were on the menu; an extra side dish with fresh vegetables and some rice. Tea was there as well both as an appetizer as well as an aperitif. While we all sat down and had dinner, the sun had moved onto the other magical lands in the west and the sky was now full of stars. It was unlike any recent starry nights that I had experienced. Deserts tend to be the best places for stargazing and Wadi Rum is no exception. But it wasn’t about the kind of place. It was also about where exactly we were. Long before any kind of man had looked up at the stars and wondered about ourselves, the men here had. Finding our smallness, sources and destinies in the stars goes a long way back for the Arab cultures and to sit at that specific spot and share some of that wonder was an enriching experience. When we finally rested for the night the desert was as cold as it was hot earlier.

The second day began with a simple breakfast of local bread and hummus. With very little driving, we were dropped off at the mouth of yet another formation that seemed like a monster with a red tongue made of a sand dune. We were told that we had to hike up that dune and through the canyon to arrive at the other end. As far as we understood the instructions we could not get lost because to get out of this labyrinth one would have to deliberately decide to do some rock climbing and avoid the obvious trail which is a river of sand that extended for a few kilometres beyond this monster’s tongue. After having ascertained that we had understood the instruction, our guide drove off in what seemed to be the opposite direction. We started climbing up the first dune which after passing through a narrow passageway opened up to a large bed of sand that was flanked on both sides by the rocks and hills; a homogeneous texture that curved to the left. With the knowledge that we could get lost only if we wanted to, we were confident to stray a bit here and there. Photography opportunities were plenty and so were the chances to sing out loud because in some of the narrow sections where the walls on either side were as high as fifty feet, the acoustical effect meant that anyone who could hum a tune, felt like a singer. We spent a good part of the entire morning session in this area before exiting it hopefully at the designated section because at some point we saw the familiar SUV drive towards us. We never asked if he found us where he expected.

After lunch and a short nap - this time really short - it was decided that we would be heading over to a place known as “Mushroom Rock” and true to its name it was to be in the shape of a mushroom where the softer base of the rock had eroded over a period of time leaving the harder sections on the top making it look like as if someone had placed a very large bulbous rock on a tiny pedestal. Our visit to this place also happened to be the first time we ran into other tourists and perhaps it was also the time of the day which invited many others here. It was interesting to note that this phenomenon had affected just one piece of rock here which made it a bit of a loner but a star attraction and a photo opportunity.

The sun was heading west again and our guide reminded us that for a logically complete trip of the Wadi Rum we would have to visit one of the sunset points that he had chosen for us. But since we had a few more hours before that we could afford one more trek, this time through the “Burrah Canyon”. This canyon has historically been fertile and has been used for growing crops in the past. There is also an old dam like structure built of stones from the Nabatean era at the narrowest end of the canyon towards the south. After dropping us at the northern point our guide drove of to the southern end where he said he would wait for us to take us to the next location. The instructions given to us were according to the confidence shown by us in previous short treks. But as luck had to be, this time we could not keep up to them.

After walking for about an hour we found ourselves lost. The reason was simply because we did not grasp that we had to actually climb over the rocks that made up the dam and thought what we saw in front of us to be the wrong way and detoured to find other routes and then failed repeatedly. This process of discovery ensured that we lost a lot of time until we figured that all we had to do was climb to a slightly higher altitude to find the way out of the canyon. Once that was actually understood, it took less than half an hour to find an opening to the south where the canyon opened up again into a large sandy valley where in the distance our impatient guide was waiting for us. Almost as soon as we had stepped back into the vehicle he was driving us to the next tea-stop which also happened to be the last attraction that we would visit before heading to the sunset point.

The “Umm Frouth Rock Bridge” as it was called is yet another natural formation where time and elements have eroded to create a bridge like structure and the adventurous ones get to climb atop the same. While my sense of adventure persisted, I lacked the time to do the same. So plans to actually attempt a climb were aborted and instead we had to take solace in enjoying the view itself and some photographs. As the sunlight waned the rock bridge formed shadows on the sandy floor while our guide, who tracked this hurriedly prepared some tea for us, all the while chatting with a compatriot who was guiding another team. I meanwhile enjoyed the last few moments of light and wonder that was the Wadi Rum and started saying our goodbyes. But not yet. We did not have to do it here. There was the actual sunset point, our guide reminded us and packed us all back into his vehicle and took us there.

Suddenly it felt as if we had entered the most touristy spots in the area. There were many vehicles parked under a large mountain, the west face of which had enough platforms at different heights where people could simply sit and watch the sun set in the distance. Many tourists had already picked their spots. Most of them perhaps going through their silent moments of solitude after spending a wonderful day in this desert. We found a little corner as well while the guide who had seen it all innumerable times instead chose to catch up with his friends at the base of the mountain. Sitting with my back against the warm rock I ran through the events of the day in my head while watching the sun set over the mountains in the distance. The redness of the setting sun was redder than ever thanks to the terrain that reflected the same colour. Chameleons scurried across to the safety of their little dens for the night. Camels yawned out of sheer boredom below while the herders sat down and rested after yet another day. In about 15 minutes it was dark again and our guide escorted us back to the vehicle and took us to a new spot he had chosen for tenting.

This time we pitched our tents in the dark and it was an effort because we had chosen a corner without much of a wind-breaker. But we managed to put it up in good time and ascertain its stability. The spot itself was excellent again for star gazing and stepping away from the tent, to see it from a distance with the stars in the backdrop was a surreal sight. However, to know that this would be the last night in the desert dampened my spirits. What was needed was some warmth to bring up the mood and that was provided by our enthusiastic guide who had again cooked up a nice meal that this time we enjoyed it sitting inside a cave. The firewood cooked meal, the fire itself and the cave setting was the perfect ending for our two days of exploring the Wadi Rum, the largest desert in Jordan. While we again chatted away with our guide and host about his country and culture in the limited language we shared we wondered what we had missed visiting. Perhaps another visit another time I thought. But till then, this will do.
