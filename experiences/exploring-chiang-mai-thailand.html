Exploring Chiang Mai, Thailand

Timeline : 2015 September
Thailand Asia Half Day Culture People City

For most people, Thailand is Bangkok and vis-a-vis. That included us too, until we had the opportunity to travel to Chiang Mai which happens to be an equally (if not more) important city in Thailand and has much to offer. We hardly had enough time to explore enough but these photographs are from one of the days we felt adventurous enough to venture into the busy city streets.

We found the iconic Tuk Tuk, not far from where we started. In fact, they were everywhere, most of them looking for rides. We had embarked on a casual walk to explore the beautiful city of Chiang Mai.
The Three Kings Monument is where we found ourselves very early. We were to later read up and understand that it commemorated the founding of the city itself. While present there though, and lacking anyone reliable to guide us, my observations were more around how familiar the statues, as well as the people it represented looked, as if pulled out from the mythologies back home.
Business was just opening up for a few of the street vendors. We came across one lady selling freshly cut fruit, which, after much deliberation, I decided to buy. Navigating the streets in Thailand was far easier than navigating the language; that was something we were still struggling with.
... Having said that, this particular sign, I did not have any problem reading. But the problem was, none of us were interested in Burgers; not in Chiang Mai by any stretch of imagination. It felt out of place in this cultured city of the east.
Speaking of culture, we came across our first Buddhist Temple: Wat Inthakhin Sadue Muang located in middle of a busy city street. Popularly called the City Pillar Temple, as it was believed to host one of the oldest city pillars. Chiang Mai is peppered with over a hundred Buddhist temples of which two or three dozens of them are ancient and significant.
Wat Lok Moli is an ancient temple in the Old City, known for its enormous 3-tiered wooden roof. The main temple hall has been well-restored, although the weathered chedi at the back shows the temple's true age. It was relatively lesser crowded and felt more tranquil.
Curiosity led us into the insides of one of them. It was familiar in some ways but yet, very different. And it had to be because the Buddhism practised in Thailand is of a different school than the ones some of us were familiar with in parts of India, especially the Himalayan Region. What was not different at all, was the calming atmosphere the setting offered, despite being right in the middle of a city
Motifs of the Buddha were found all over the place, but ...
... what really surprised me were the statues of Lord Ganesha, I found just outside one of the Buddhist Temples, one of them in a dancing pose, ubiquitous paunch included.
All said and done, Chiang Mai, like many cities of the East, is still two-worlds. Buddhist Temples and Buddhas shared space with modern supermarket chains and wall Grafitti. We decided to break for lunch at the Free Bird cafe, a vegan place with some amazing food surrounded by messages of love and peace
Tuk tuks shared space with large SUVs. And all of them were sharing space with a number of two wheelers that would snake through all kinds of traffic.
But then, an untimely afternoon shower seemed to completely spoil it for the two wheeler riders. This was the monsoons, so perhaps it had to be expected. But considering it was a sunny morning, we were surprised by the sudden turn of events.
The safe confines of a four wheeler helped some. But the downpour had disrupted what was an ordinary morning for most people and cut short our exploration of this wonderful city that we had just chanced upon.
