Staying in a remote East Ugandan Village, Africa
Staying in a remote East Ugandan Village, Africa

Timeline : 2018 February
Uganda Africa Multi Day People

“Is it true that women are burnt alive for dowry in India?” Mai asked with fear in her eyes and a palm covering her mouth. She did not mean to offend me. The innocence in her voice and her genuine concern for her sisters in India made me offer a simple, plain answer. “Yes, it does happen even now in some villages” I tried to calm her down. “I heard that in India women have to pay dowry money? And not the other way around?” her line of questions on women’s empowerment or the lack of it continued. Then she shared with me rather proudly the story of how her husband was the one who paid her a dowry that consisted of four cows and a small piece of land during their wedding. And so went our conversations till late in the evening while the food was slowly being cooked on the fire, giving out irresistible aroma. I had become part of their family for the seven days I spent at their home. “Mai” means mother in Lugisu, a local language spoken around this region.

My travels had taken me to an inconspicuous village at the foothills of the majestic Mt. Elgon in eastern Uganda. The one time this village was in news was due to a devastating landslide that had occurred a few years ago. Surrounded by lush green mountains, this small village called Bukali was in a valley with a river running across it, there were a few houses with coffee, plantain, vegetables growing abundantly.

I was keen to hear her stories, as much as I was to learn cooking from her. She could produce the most delicious dishes and make it seem effortless. When I started peeling raw bananas for Matoke - a cooked, mashed up raw plantain dish, I realized how difficult it was. We cooked in the courtyard on a firewood stove, under the stars, sipping chilled local beer. Coming from India, I could relate to her stories, many of which drew similarities from people back home. We were both colonized by the British to begin with. This was also the reason we could converse in a common tongue. Mai explained to me about the population issue, the unwanted pregnancies, the lack of awareness about HIV, orphaned children and poverty. She told me that one of the children playing around in her courtyard that morning was not her grandchild but an orphan whose father was unknown and the mother died of AIDS. She had generously taken him into her fold and was bringing him up as one of her grandchildren. A shy, mute whom I had met earlier was HIV positive and Mai told me that she had no family, so she would come to work and eat here.

That night I could barely sleep. I was thinking about the cozy life I led, the arrogance and selfishness that inherently comes with affluence. And how here in this small village I was humbled. I was not preached about their way of life or taught human values. Yet, subtly, by living with them, how easily I was considered as one of their own, how they were kind enough to let me into their homes, to share with me their stories openly, trustingly. They believed it to be their duty to take care of others who were lesser fortunate, and share whatever little they have with others. Isn’t this what we call “dharma” back home? At that moment I felt the boundaries blur, the differences fading and a deep bond connecting us. I felt as though I knew them forever.
