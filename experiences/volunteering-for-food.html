“Why we chose Bunda, or should I say, why Bunda chose us?” our host Aysha spoke of her reason for choosing to make this sleepy village her home. I sat in front of her nervously, hoping that the voice was clearly recording and we would complete the interview in a single take. She was dressed up for the interview, had rehearsed the questions, but we chose to keep the interview itself spontaneous, impromptu and she was eager to get it right. Of course, it did not all come out perfectly the first time, or the second even, but we persisted and extracted pieces of it to make sure it fits into our narrative we had chalked out. We wanted to make it seem professional as well as keep well within our self-imposed deadline of around three days end-to-end. This short five to six minute promo video of the Tembea Mara at Balili Village, an eco-tourism resort outside the Serengeti Ndabaka Gate, western Tanzania, was one of our several volunteering jobs we had got during our Tanzanian travel. We were fortunate that there were no other guests during our entire stay giving us complete freedom to shoot the various components involved. The beautiful setting, the tastefully done interiors, the large dining hall and kitchen, the various creepy crawlies and critter around and the friendly staff, all of these were featured in our short film.

When we wrote to Aysha before leaving India, little did we know that we would take up a project such as planning, shooting, editing and completing a promo video with our limited resources - a DSLR and a travel laptop. From our description about our interests and skills, she had decided to ask us to shoot a video and promote her small business online. Not a small task, given that we were already tired from our long travel crossing borders and arriving in this small little village. Although what we had hoped for was a room to stay or a place to pitch our tent and some food, we realized that our host Aysha had planned much more for us. She wanted to treat us like special guests, cook up some delicious food for us, show us around and present to us the complete “Tembea Mara” (which meant Walk the Mara) experience as she had envisioned it and in return hoped that we would come up with a good quality promotional material for her. It was very generous of her to trust us, just amateurs, to come up with something worthwhile, ensuring that we were well taken care of. We were treated as friends, not as guests, not the least as just volunteers. While we enjoyed our hospitality, we were constantly thinking of how best to capture all of it and present it, so the camera was always at my side, capturing what we saw and experienced.

One of the experiences that Aysha offers to her guests was a short hike to the Balili Hill overseeing her property. An easy hike through the rock formations and an inconspicuous path leads us within an hour to the summit, a vantage point offering birds eye view of the entire village, the plains of Serengeti far west, watching the sun set into the vast, expansive Lake Victoria, one of the largest lakes in Africa. The hill formed a stunning backdrop for her property and we wanted to highlight its importance as Aysha mentioned to us that when they came to see this property, it was the hill that attracted her the most. Bunda was not yet a tourist town, yet, it was growing fast. After Tembea Mara, they had seen a couple of other resorts coming up close to the hill, giving them competition. But we have seen so many businesses and know that the key difference between a decent stay and a memorable one was the staff, the host and how genuine they were. And I thought that Aysha and her team at Tembea Mara were extremely courteous and helpful. Moreover, I felt that the proximity to Serengeti West gate which was a huge draw, especially in the dry season when hordes of tourists and photographers descend at a time to capture the wildebeests getting slayed by the hungry crocodiles as their annual migration crosses the Grumeti River, was the highlight. Tourists who typically start and end their game drives within the airstrips inside the National Park now had the opportunity for an additional experiences such as interaction with the indigenous Maasai tribes who have for centuries walked this land and co-habited with the wildlife of Maara.

The agamas were a striking feature at the resort both as motifs and as living creatures crawling on walls. As we started chasing the agamas to get a good shot, I could see many smaller creatures, all of which became a part of the video. The resort itself was aesthetically built integrating several earthy elements and smaller detailing that I admired and captured. The rooms were sturdy, a little distance apart from one another and had a small garden each. Due to bad weather on one of the nights, we found our tent was flooded and we rescued our belongings in time and moved into one of the guest rooms. Again, the generosity of Aysha ensured that we were not left out in the cold to fend for ourselves, even as we did not want to impose. Although it was not a part of the plan, we got to experience all their accommodation options.
