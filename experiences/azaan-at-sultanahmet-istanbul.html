Azaan at Sultanahmet, Istanbul
Azaan at Sultanahmet, Istanbul

Timeline : 2018 April
Turkey Asia Half Day Culture People Historical

Walking the crowded streets of Sultanahmet late evening, I was trying to negotiate a road crossing, just as a tram passed the street. The wind was chill yet filled with the aroma of the food from the roadside restaurants. Just then, I heard the most melodious tune somewhere far that made me freeze. I just stood there mesmerized, holding on to every single sound that came out from this singer. The words “Allahu Akhbar”, probably heard several times in my lifetime, had never before sounded so beautiful, so profound. As though a conversation was on, there was a reply from another voice, a little away but equally melodious. For an entire minute or two, I was lost. It did not matter to me that I was standing in the middle of a busy street; I might have blocked traffic; I might have looked stupid to onlookers. But all I knew was that I was transported to another world, far away from the present one. A world that was filled with beauty, spirituality and music. I could feel a tingling sensation through my body, I had goosebumps on my arms and could not help smiling.

Before coming to Istanbul, I had read bucket lists written by people containing “Azaan from Blue mosque and Hagia Sofia” as one of the items. I was amused to read it and had wondered how something as regular as an Azaan could become a bucket list item, especially in Istanbul, a city that is so unique, so rich that there could be a million to-do items here, that might not be fulfilled in one’s lifetime. I had brushed it aside and had almost forgotten about it, till this moment.

It made every other azaan I had ever listened to obsolete. Never again would I enjoy Azaan from anybody else. The next time, I made sure I found a nice spot on one of the benches between Hagia Sofia and Blue Mosque well before the prayer call and sat there in anticipation. Again, the most melodious of tunes emanated from the Hagia Sofia, replied to by the Blue Mosque, and yet again, I was swept away. It soon became one of my most cherished of experiences, not just in Istanbul, but anywhere else as well.

A simple call to prayer, to make people assemble to the mosque to pray, rendered five times a day, could be either mundane or exhilarating. The atmosphere here, the beautiful structures, their history, their mysticism and the best singers engaged for the prayer call certainly makes this one of the top bucket list items. Later, I heard that the singers here are one of the highest paid, employed specially for this prayer call. Singing, I have heard from experts, could be of three levels, the first that makes you just recognize the tune, the notes, the words, you are aware of the song, and would hum it. The next level, where you feel good about it, you appreciate it, you admire the singing, the technicalities, the raaga, the complexity, the manoeuvers deftly achieved by the singer. The third level, which all singers should aspire to reach is experiential. It is hard to describe it. You do not say it was good, it leaves you speechless, it touches you, it transports you to another world, for a few moments you lose yourself. And when someone asks about it, you stutter, you smile, you feel unable to express yourself in words. And this is the ultimate goal of music, of any art, to take each of us to the ultimate truth. After which there could be nothing left to converse about.
