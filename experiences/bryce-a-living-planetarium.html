Bryce : A Living Planetarium
Bryce : A Living Planetarium

Timeline : 2018 October
United States of America North America Half Day Nature

It was not easy to comprehend the uniqueness of what was going on. There he was, a seemingly ordinary Park Ranger as he stood with his hands inside the pockets of his jacket to beat the chill of the night while he gave us a short lecture on what we were about to do and witness. I looked around. We were twenty of us who had signed up for the event, standing around in a circle. Again, a rather ordinary looking circle in an open section of the spacious parking lot near the visitor centre of the Bryce Canyon National Park. What wasn’t ordinary about all this was the sky that was above. It seemed to defy all that I had until then assumed, and insisted on mesmerizing me with a dazzling display. It wasn’t that carefully crafted pitch dark where we bump into each other, and yet the clarity offered by the dome of the sky above was so much that I could spot a star cluster here or tell-tale signs of a nebula there simply by looking up, as the Ranger concluded his instructions.

It was only due to providence that we ended up at Bryce Canyon. We were on our way to its rather famous cousin, that was the Grand Canyon National Park but a combination of the setting sun and tiring body seemed to magically guide us towards Bryce which began as a stopover but as we read up about it turned into a destination to consider. The fact that Bryce Canyon has one of the darkest skies in all of North America meant that it was not just me who got excited about stargazing here. Despite the fact that Bryce offered spectacular scenery which included natural amphitheatres, rock formations known as hoodoos, and casual trekking, it is regarded as a hot destination by a lot of people who simply liked to stare at the sky, knowing very well that if any magic was expected to happen that was entirely outside our control, it was up there. I was pretty pleased to find one of the last few spots when I signed up earlier in the day for the lecture session and guided stargazing activity for that night.

The Ranger had setup his telescope and asked us to form a line, which we did. I could feel a gentle breeze that forced me to open up the flask I was carrying and let the warmth of the water soothe my throat as I took a sip and closed it again. The only light in the parking lot was a medium-tall lamp post that by convention and regulation had a covered top to ensure lowest light pollution. Rest of the light came from the warm and dim interiors of the visitor centre and the lodge where through the glass walls I could see some people dining. As the line moved ahead, I waited in anticipation to see what the Ranger had setup for observation. Eventually I got to the meaningful end of the line. One look through the eyepiece and I instinctively smiled. He had started with the best. The Andromeda Galaxy; at 2.5 million light-years away, one of the farthest objects to gaze at in the night sky. Farthest object in the darkest skies made a lot of sense. That kind of distance also meant that I was looking at our past 2.5 million years ago which also made it the oldest thing I would look at. Darkest, Farthest, Oldest made even more sense and all of this worth it.

Having kicked off the event with a big bang - pun fully intended - the Ranger moved the telescope and pointed it almost in the exact opposite direction and affixed it in succession on two planets that seemed to be in a race that night towards the Western Horizon; Jupiter and Saturn. The really dark skies ensured that they were looking at their distinctive best even without a telescope but I was all excited to look at them, enlarged and focused. The peering into what should be called a time-machine did the expected. I was as gleeful as a child would be. But I hoped that all this was a warm up, even if it did nothing for the body. It was. The ranger moved onto a few more Messier objects but it also had to be noted that it wasn’t a particularly eventful night. No comets in the sky on their routine visits or celestial vagabonds who sometimes get adventurous on their interstellar highways. But what I was glad to learn was that stargazing was an activity the National Park and its patrons pursued very seriously. So much so that they had an Astronomy Festival each year. I mentally took a note to visit again during the events and for detailed sessions as I temporarily made a connection once with another friend from the sky, one eye excitedly pressed against the eyepiece and the other one shut. Someone in the background was already suggesting the next object to look for before we end the session.
