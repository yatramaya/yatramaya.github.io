Lounge-birding at Sadiri lodge

Timeline : 2019 August
Bolivia South America Full Day Forest

We got a glimpse of what we would experience after 3 days when we stepped into Sadiri Lodge briefly to put away some our clothes that we wouldn’t be needing during the camping. The lodge was located on a hill, the steep slope of which was lined with a stone-pitched stairway that we arduously climbed. The entrance of the prominent looking building, completely built of wood, led to the dining hall with a large balcony overlooking the dramatic mountain ranges of Amazon rainforests. We saw hummingbirds regularly perching there to drink from the feeders put out for them. Later, we came to appreciate the lodge even better after three days of roughing it out in the jungle at various spots. By then, we had roughed it out quite a bit and the lodge was a luxurious abode, keeping us well protected from insects and yet giving us the forest sounds and breeze. The accommodation was at independent cabanas built at considerable distance from each other; six of them in total. Each cabin had its own pathway, lights, balcony and a beautiful room with nice warm beds; also, most importantly, clean bathrooms. All structures seemed to be constructed out of good quality wood sourced from the same place and built to last. But all that wood around did not mean dark, moist interiors which have not known light. The cabins had huge glass windows and doors all around to give us a good view of everything outside and yet we have the necessary privacy due to its relatively remote location from any other structures. The dining hall at the main building was at a distance and a good 10 minute walk which included a respectable climb up the stairs. The dining area moonlighted as an observatory, if one has the patience and time to sit and watch wildlife.

The experience of staying at the lodge for three full days contrasted the first three days of back-country that we had completed. The trails around the lodge were relatively well marked and treaded (except for a few places). But having said that, this did not translate automatically to “easy” as the terrain was hilly, involving steep climbs in several places with the added bonus of leading to some breathtaking views of the mountains. The walks were definitely worth it and we did spot many unique wildlife including a lot of birds. The two mammal sightings we had were very close the lodge - the Olingo (during one of the night walks) and the Tamarin monkey. It was apparent that the lodge being located in a remote area with hardly any human activity, combined with the fact that we were the only guests at the lodge, made it possible to spot wildlife at close range. If one needed additional aids, the lodge also had a watch tower which could be used to view the birds; we had an owl sighting here on one of the evenings. Although the lodge tempted us to sit and laze around, it was always well worth walking around and hard work was well rewarded. The hot shower at the end of the walk and the gastronomical experience at meal-time felt a little more earned when we sweated it out and worked hard before.

Coming to food, we were treated to many traditional dishes, prepared using local ingredients like peanuts, yucca, plantain, quinoa, fresh-fruits and vegetables. Everything was done with a level of expertise and a measure of confidence that left both the chefs as well as the customers beaming. And thanks to that, we were just looking forward to the meals; one meal to another, with nothing on the menu repeated in the six days! This was quite amusing considering that we were not really here for the food. Breakfast was a large spread of fruits, coffee, tea, juice, bread, spreads, cheese, granola and yogurt; everything fresh. The lunches and dinners, not to be left behind were relatively larger affairs. We had indicated we were vegetarians and we were pleasantly surprised with the knowledge and expertise the chef had and the extra effort she always put in to make us tasty dishes that were totally vegetarian. Our guide Ricardo and translator/organizer Gera, always made a point to eat with us, leading to interesting conversations that gave us a lot of insight into the lodge, the community and the amazon as a whole.

During our dinner conversations, I gathered information about the history of the lodge and the involvement of the local community in eco-tourism initiatives which eventually birthed the Sadiri lodge. The community which owns this initiative and on whose land the lodge was built is called the San Jose de Uchupiamonas, a settlement of about 100 families some 100 miles away from the lodge, the same village that we visited on our first day itself. Ricardo recounted the hard work and dedication of their founder, Ruth Alipaz, who is from the same community and had become an entrepreneur. It was due to her efforts, that this eco-tourism initiative took off, focused on bird-watching. Essentially they had two goals, of saving the forests from the logging mafia and of financially benefiting the families of the community. All the staff at Sadiri - the guides, chefs, helping staff - are from the same community who had been given top notch training in hospitality and bird watching. We found this model to be a win-win provided that they maintain good standards for guests and also ensure that the ecosystem was strictly protected with least disturbance or nuisance to the abundant life around.
