The Whirling Dervishes of Turkey

Timeline : 2018 April
Turkey Asia Europe Half Day People Culture City

Istanbul’s reputation as a historical city with great culture and tradition was one of the reasons we decided to generously let it occupy a greater part of our Turkey trip. While reading about the culture, I came upon its Sufi tradition, known as the Mevlevi Order; inspired by “Mevlana” Rumi, the famous Islamic mystical poet and spiritual master. Sufism is a form of Islam which emphasizes the inward search for God through direct connect and promotes realization of God through love and music. The Mevlevi Order we see in Turkey has kept this tradition alive with the spiritual “Sema Ceremony” taking place at various mosques and cultural centres. The origin of this order can be traced back 800 years ago at Konya in Turkey. When I heard about this ceremony that brings the participants closer to God by denouncing materialistic world and transcend into the spiritual realm through mind and love, I went in search of it. Music plays a central part in the Sema ceremony that involve dancers in white robes and conical hats, famously known as the Whirling Dervishes. It is a spiritual practice - privately held at Mevlevi houses - known as Mevlevihane where dancers would learn about ethics, code of conduct, prayer, poetry, dance and music for about 1000 days. When Turkey leant towards secularism, the mevlevihanes closed down. After that, in 1950, the Turkish Government began allowing performances but only in public.

Just hearing about and reading about the ceremony sent us on a “Sema-hunt” of sorts. Initially, we were adamant that we shouldn’t settle for anything less than an authentic experience. We figured that the Sema still happens in Konya - the place where it started - but knew that we could not include Konya into our unforgivably tight itinerary. When we force-fitted it in, it couldn’t be accommodated on the exact day of the week when the ceremony takes place (Thursdays). Eventually we succumbed. We decided to settle for the next best location for viewing this performance, the Hodjapasha Cultural Centre in Istanbul. I didn’t know what to expect but I with an open mind. Even though the Sema is a spiritual ceremony performed by the Dervishes to establish their connect with the spiritual realm, it has been adapted and moulded into the form of a performance where the audience can view this in silence without causing any disturbance to the ceremony. No children under 7, no mobile phone, no photography and absolutely no movement in and out of the hall once the performance starts. I was happy to see that most of the people adhered to these rules and maintained the sanctity of this ceremony.

The lighting painted the entire hall purple and the ambience was pregnant with a promise of something surreal. 550 years ago the hall we were in used to be a Hamam (bathing house). The crowd, the setting and my prior knowledge reminded me that what was about to happen was a “show”, a “performance” and not the actual ceremony where the participants are known to become euphoric, faint or express their emotions freely. But I also knew that it was worth attending to get to know this unique culture. The unimposing size of the venue was to ensure that the performance was expected to be an intimate experience for a limited audience. I felt that the cosy space and the proximity to the performers enhanced the experience. Then the performance began with a band of musicians; traditional instruments consisting of a flute, drums and cymbals announced themselves. The dervishes entered and spread a sheepskin on the wooden floor after which they removed their black cloaks, signifying the shedding of material world. They were all in pure white clothing. The music was soulful and it drew me into the world of Sufism. The musicians, in perfect tune and harmony, played a crucial role in the entire experience. After the initial discomfort I had because of the knowledge that this was only a “show”, I was drawn into the world of mysticism and was lost in a sea of flowing white robes and the accompanying music.

There was something, something surreal to see the dervishes tilt their heads at an angle, lift their right hand towards the sky and lower the other one towards the ground and turn on their left foot using the right one to propel them into endless rotations. The posture of the hands, as I understand, is a passage for divine energy to flow through. The entire process induces a meditative state to aid their communion with the divine; the spiritual destroying the material. Their movements made them seem like they were floating inches above the ground I feared that, if I wasn’t attentive enough, I might miss their ascension into the sky. I was so engrossed in this beautiful experience that an hour felt like a few minutes and before I realized, the “show” was over. Real Sema ceremonies do not have a time limit. It depends on the time a person needs to lose their identity and ego. The performance we witnessed had a distinct beginning and end. It left me thirsting for more; probably another time, another place. I was satisfied, in fact, overwhelmed by this experience experience itself. As the soulful music lingered on and I walked out of the venue, some unknown warmth had wrapped around me; that much I am sure of. There are no photographs or videos of that wonderful night; some memories just have to exist within one’s mind. In fact, I can’t think of a better tribute to that experience.
