Indigo Dyeing : Phrae, Thailand
Indigo Dyeing : Phrae, Thailand

Timeline : 2015 September
Thailand Asia Half Day Culture People

The various shades of blue on either side of the main road told us that we had reached our destination - Ban Thung Hong, the Blue Village as it is popularly known. A group of six friends with a shared passion for indigo, we had set out to explore the source of this beautiful colour. True to its name, this quaint village, just outside the city of Phrae is a relic from the Kingdom of Old Siam. With its old teak houses, its small population mainly engaged with the Indigo dyeing, it has opened its doors to keen visitors who are willing to look further than the touristy cities and beaches of Thailand.

The fascinating sight of a cloth, dipped in a foul smelling large pot filled with a greenish sludge, on exposure to sun, turning from greenish to deep blue slowly, is nothing short of magic. We were enthralled by the passionate demonstration of the entire dyeing process by an elderly lady, adept at the skill she has practised for several decades, passed on to her through generations. Although the entire narration was in Thai, I could connect with each step of the process without much difficulty. The large earthen pots filled with Indigo liquid, the unmistakable stench of the fermented broth, the greenish algae-coloured sludge that creates the magic, all seemed familiar. Separated by geographies and political boundaries, there is such similarity in the art of Indigo dyeing that Thailand and India share. I recounted a similar demonstration of the dyeing process in a small village in Karnataka where natural indigo vat dyeing was practised. The visuals were overlapping in my mind; the language barrier became blurred as my mind started recollecting the narration from my earlier experience in a familiar language. I was eager to tell her about my country and its similar practices, but I smiled helplessly, gesturing to her in approval and pointing at the indigo shirt I was wearing.

“Mo Hom” as the fabric is known comes from the Lanna language (northern Thailand); “Mo” means pot and “Hom” is the name of the plant from which indigo is extracted. The process is completely natural and uses lime to aid the fermentation process. The leaves of the indigo plant is immersed in an earthen pot for about 3 days. Then lime is added and the mixture is stirred vigorously to aid oxygenation. The indigo sludge forms at the bottom of the pot and can be used as the dye. In the village in Karnataka, the process starts with dried indigo cakes (blue in colour) which are immersed in water to which lime is added and left to ferment. The fabric or the yarn to be dyed is then dipped in this solution and dried in sunlight, during which is goes from a greenish colour to deep blue. The process was demonstrated beautifully on a stole. Then we went to the block printing area where hot wax was brewing in a large pot from which wooden blocks dipped in the liquid were skilfully printed on a stole. This technique called batik, which is also widely practised in India, is a resist dyeing technique. This means that parts of the fabric where the wax is printed (in a continuous design) is not exposed to the indigo dye when the fabric is dipped into the indigo vat. The wax is later washed away in hot water to reveal the white spaces unravelling the design against the deep blue background.

What we witnessed was what is known as fabric dyeing in indigo. What I have earlier seen in Karnataka is yarn dyeing, where the yarn is first dyed in indigo and then used on a handloom to weave it into fabric. What I missed on my short trip to Phrae and later came to know is that weaving is also common in Ban Thung Hong. The cotton fabric weaving is done on traditional wooden frame looms by women, much like the ones back home that I have seen. The cotton yarns are dyed in indigo and then woven into beautiful patterns on the loom at the Thai Puan Weaving Group in the village. A missed opportunity, something for another time, perhaps.

The senior lady’s daughter ran a boutique at the front of the dyeing house. Since she spoke English, she patiently listened to our enthusiastic questions and tried to answer them. Understanding the process and appreciating the hard work behind each piece of fabric we saw at the shop, we were blown away by the various designs that were on offer. There was little doubt on where most of the gifts would be picked up for friends back home. Having filled our bags with a varied collection of indigo products and our minds with pleasant memories etched in deep blue, we felt our shared thirst for indigo quenched in this little village.
