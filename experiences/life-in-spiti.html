Life in Spiti

Timeline : 2017 October
India Asia Half Day People Culture

I sat there as though in a trance; watching how the horizontal yarn moved through the hundreds of threads that were hanging along the frame of the loom. Her hands moving rhythmically in a quick, yet, skilful way made me wonder how it might feel to sit there in solitude, patiently working on the pattern. It was meditative. I had a hundred questions to ask her. I hesitated. Would she get disturbed by my interruption and make a mistake? I just sat there watching, while thinking about her difficult life. Waking up early in the morning, tending to the sheep, milking the cattle, feeding them, then starting up a firewood stove to make some tea for her family, then working on the farm till noon, coming back home to prepare lunch, then clean the house, the utensils, finally, after dusk, when it is too dark outside to work, inside her house, she sat there working on her woollen rugs. And, if all this was not a handful, she had the homestay and the guests to take care of, entertain and feed. She was clearly running the household and had trained her eldest daughter to help around with her work. The men of the household would go out searching for work and come back at dusk to eat and drink. Some regulars at her small, informal restaurant included a lorry driver, who would eat and sleep at the homestay whenever he came to Dhankar. In winters, all this was made much harder with the subzero temperatures and the limited resources. Every piece of wood had to be cut and stored before it snowed to ensure food can be cooked. Grains had to be dried and stored away to have enough food to last the winter. Without guests bringing in any income during most of the winter, any money earned during the summer months had to be saved and stretched.

“Are you going to sell it?” I asked, finally breaking the silence, referring to the rug she was weaving. I wondered if parting with it could bring in some additional income. Although, I wasn’t sure about the price it might get her or how she could have access to markets. “No, I am just weaving it for my family”, she said, half-smilingly. I was surprised as well as satisfied. I was humbled by the fact that that she wasn’t even considering putting it up for sale. Anything of such value was to be created, cherished, and passed on through generations. She explained that it was family heirloom. She was weaving it for her daughter’s wedding; it was an important component of festivities and tradition. She was weaving it with a lot of love and longing; like how an expectant mother would weave mittens for her not-yet-born baby. She hoped that her daughter would be married soon to a good family. “I make the wool from the sheep, black and white, then dye it in colours and use it for the rugs. It is pure sheep wool.” It would easily be priced at twenty to thirty thousand rupees, I estimated. Anything handmade and of high quality wool was definitely of great value, if presented to the right market. She did not seem surprised when I quoted the estimated price, it seems like she already knew the value. It was just that it did not matter to her since she did not look at it as a commodity that was on sale. She was content with her life and her earnings.

As I watched intently and tried to film her weaving the carpet, she graciously smiled and showed me how she was getting the pattern done. As seasoned weavers who are adept at their skill, she was not relying on any markings, she just knew when she had to insert the right colour yarn so as to build the pattern. She probably envisioned the design beforehand in her mind, or she made it up spontaneously as she progressed, I was not sure. But those were just technicalities. What mattered was her dedication and love towards this carpet she would hold close to her heart. It reminded me of the film Kancheepuram, where a poor weaver who has woven thousands of expensive silk sarees all his life could not afford one for his own daughter’s wedding when the time came. When most weavers suffer such a piteous life, it was heart-warming to see such confidence and self-restraint in a woman who did not necessarily lead a cozy life. But it was important for her to weave everyday, to fulfil her responsibility. Nobody told her to do so, but she just wanted to.
