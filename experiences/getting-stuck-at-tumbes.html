Getting Stuck at Tumbes
Getting Stuck at Tumbes

Timeline : 2019 September
Peru South America Multi Day City Culture Historical Market People

If getting to Tumbes wasn’t easy, getting out wasn’t any easier either. As I sat staring at the news-reporter on the TV and reading the news-ticker which was in English reporting on the riots in Ecuador, I felt completely helpless and hopeless. All the local TV channels were reporting on the same topic and no amount of switching channels was going to change anything for us. We were simply destined to be stuck in that mostly unknown border town till something changed radically in the next few days or we retreated in defeat. I wondered if it could have been better. The warning signs were posted early and in hindsight, I think we could have abandoned this plan much earlier.

It was about a week before we actually made it to Tumbes. We were at Chiclayo which was supposed to be a minor stop after we had completed a large chunk of our adventures in Peru and the next item in our itinerary was to visit the Galapagos islands which officially belonged to the country of Ecuador. If things went as per plan, we would experience a cherish-able cruise around the islands and then we would fly out of Guayaquil to New York, a ticket that we had obtained for a decent price and was also our proof of exiting the continent itself which was a necessity in the visa affairs through this entire backpacking trip. I distinctly remember taking it lightly as news of riots appeared on TV the first time, when we went and booked an international bus service that should take us from Chiclayo to Guayaquil via Tumbes border crossing. The riots began as protests in Quito against the revoking of fuel subsidies but soon became a pan-country event. We felt the first jolt when we checked out of the hotel after saying our goodbyes to the friendly staff and arrived at the bus station only to be told “Lo Siento” (sorry) and - our money returned - citing that the bus that we were supposed to board had been cancelled. The walk from the bus station to the hotel again, the explanations, the patronizing looks and sympathy from the hotel staff; all that was painful. The journey through Peru and even the rest of the continent till that point was more or less as per plan up until. That incident should have warned us and put some sense into us right then, but it didn’t.

As the days passed, confidence returned; one could say overconfidence because it only got us until the border. Just a few days after the first attempt and despite the fact that there was nothing positive being reported in the news from Ecuador, we attempted booking tickets with another bus company that claimed that the borders had indeed opened and they would ply the route. Galapagos beckoned! I was excited again as we boarded a bus that was full of anxious faces, many others who perhaps like us had postponed their journeys. Nevertheless, most of that trip was in good spirits. i.e. until at midnight when the stewardess walked the aisle and woke up people and informed in Spanish that the bus would go no further. I had just been woken up and added to that was still not confident with the language, especially in crisis, so at first I assumed that we had just stopped for border crossing. But my impatience slowly turned into dread and horror when we were informed that the bus was going to be indefinitely parked because it could go no further than Tumbes. The bus was parked in the yard belong to the office of the travel company and we were all asked to sit in the waiting lounge. The time was around 1am and Tumbes was just a sleeping town outside that we should have only been passing through.

When the darkness outside gave way to light, dust, jostling, people and eventually traffic, we however, were still there. We continued to be there when we accepted part of our payment and when they called us all for a meeting and announced that “this is it, there is nothing we can do”. We refused their offer to take us in the same bus back to Chiclayo (for no additional payment). We accepted a refund and walked with our backpacks looking for a place to stay. I started feeling cheated and denied. But there wasn’t anything I could do really other than hope for the best and find a place to wait.

Tumbes, which I probably wouldn’t have even remembered after a few months into my return home thus became an important part of my travel memories. We had chosen a lodging that was close to a Mall which was at the peripheries of a Plaza that was the centre piece of the town. We chose the Mall only because unlike our experiences at other towns and cities in Peru we found that at Tumbes our weak point became a severe disadvantage; language. English was spoken lesser and lesser and our broken Spanish was not passing off well. Inside the Mall were a few shops that seemed to have folks who could talk to desperate tourists like us washed up in nondescript towns. At the bare minimum, eating wouldn’t be a hindrance. The Mall had a few food joints that sold familiar items. I wasn’t in the best of moods and had become lesser than open to experience food or conversations so rote familiarity such as the acceptance of universal realities such as pizzas and burgers would be appreciated in this circumstance.

Since Tumbes was not particularly known for any ancient monuments or ruins, or was not part of the tourist circuit of Peru, we found the locals here went about their lives without taking any special note of a handful of foreigners who were passing through. We could experience a normal, regular, uneventful time living like locals - we even considered watching a film (the Spanish dubbed version of Joker). We could walk the streets without being pursued by hawkers, we could walk into a restaurant and eat ice creams and pastries along with the locals, have a drink or a coffee watching TV and not be “sold” anything. Most tourists even if they cross over from Peru to Ecuador avoid the land crossing at Tumbes as it is notorious for some petty and serious crimes; getting mugged was all too common in a zone unoriginally known as the no-mans-land between Peru and Ecuador.

In the evenings and when we weren’t watching the news on TV we went on strolls around the plaza and also along streets radiating away from it. Tumbes revealed itself to us very slowly. We started our explorations for food and found a small hole in the wall cake shops that we liked and panaderias that sold fresh bread every day. We also found cheaper and better accommodations, one of which we would eventually shift into. We found friendly people and bistros that served freshly cooked meals and a good drink. When feeling a bit more adventurous we also ended up at places that was visited only by the locals for eating dishes only they could decipher. I remember a lunch place where we mistakenly assumed to have conveyed everything that we needed to convey to a very friendly waiter who then placed a plateful of items in front of us, most of it containing meat. Out of sheer embarrassment mixed with courtesy I picked morsels of it, parts of it that were not meat and discreetly allowed the rest to go waste.

Eventually, switching TV channels or lodges did not help the cause. The riots in Ecuador stretched beyond everyone’s expected timelines. The news-reporters looked bored themselves providing the same update day in and day out. I started wondering if I would even have the confidence to enter Ecuador if they actually let us in. I dreaded the possibility of getting stuck in the middle of riots at Guayaquil or Quito instead without being able to get out to New York. It would have been of no use if we even made it to the mainland and not to the Galapagos, as desired. We roamed the streets of Tumbes, hoping against hope, to hear some positive news. Instead, our decision was sealed when out of the blue, standing in the queue of a supermarket, a friendly man just behind us, advised us not to go into Ecuador. He said, in Spanish, that it was better to be on safer territory than get into a riot ridden country. We took his word as a sign from some greater power and booked the next bus that would take us back to Chiclayo.
