Yellowstone where the Bisons still roam free

Timeline : 2019 October
United States of America North America Half Day Nature Wildlife

Vast stretches of grasslands till the horizon, spreading a greenish yellow carpet. The mountains far away decorate the edges of the grasslands. This land was owned by large herds of bison a long time ago till the western civilization set foot on the continent. The bison were bigger compared to their relatively smaller descendants we still see at Yellowstone today. The herds were larger too, of a scale that probably we might find it hard to imagine. They ruled this land; they knew how to survive the harshest winters bordering on starvation and had thrived; that is till the modern human came with their guns. The bisons were almost wiped out; so were the native Indians who had co-habited this place peacefully with the beasts. The landscape here demands much larger animals; probably the dinosaurs would have been more suited. The bisons look like tiny brown dots scattered across like particles of dust, their presence not as imposing as it had seemed in my imagination. Yet these bisons are the most striking feature of Yellowstone. Their grit, their persistence, their survival is also in so many ways the story of the park.

The bisons go about their lives slowly, calmly most of the time without minding the flurry of activities around. Once in a while they get into fights among themselves, especially the males, which might become a spectacle for the passing tourists. The young calves are especially endearing to observe, full of antics and playfulness. Sometimes a large herd gets onto the road causing a traffic jam that might last for a few minutes. We were lucky to have been caught in one such traffic jam. Without the usual frustration and road rage that might accompany a traffic jam on a city road, we stopped the car, took in a breath of fresh air and watched the breathtaking landscape as we waited patiently with our camera for the celebrities to arrive. It was early in the morning and the sun had only begun to warm the chilly night air. The couple in the car just in front of us used the time wisely to brush their teeth, change clothes and get ready all the while keeping an eye out for the bisons. The herd was scattered once they intersected the vehicles on the road. Camera shy as all animals generally are, they quickly ducked and disappeared into the meadows on the other side. A few crossed just in front of our car. I could not just observe them up close, I could even smell them. Although slow and laid back at most times, they quickened their pace and appeared a little jittery as they passed among the vehicles, understandably.

We visited Yellowstone in fall, when there was still ample food for the bisons. To witness their real grit and character we had to see them in the peak of winter, when the food dwindles, when the snow storms threaten to sweep them off their feet, when the temperatures drop to several degrees below zero. This is when they transform themselves - grow thicker fur, lose body weight, huddle together and control their metabolism to conserve as much energy as possible to tide over the cold, dark winter days. I remembered a documentary film I had watched earlier about the beauty of Yellowstone and I was struck by the transformation the wildlife of the park go through in preparation for the harsh weather. The bisons survive on very less vegetation and start losing a large part of their body weight as they spend the winter months huddling together, foraging and braving the snowstorms. Come spring, they slowly start regaining their lost fat and start preparing themselves for the more fertile season where they start their mating ritual and the raising of new life within the herd.

Once hunted indiscriminately, these bisons of Yellowstone have survived difficult times and lived to tell the tale. Yellowstone was one of the first National Parks setup in the US and this has provided the much needed protection for the bisons to roam freely in a fraction of land that they once owned and roamed. But they have made this park their home, where they continue to live and thrive.
