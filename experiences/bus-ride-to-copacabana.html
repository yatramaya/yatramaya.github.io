From the Bolivian Highlands of La Paz, to Copacabana

Timeline : 2019 August
Bolivia South America Half Day Roadtrip

There are many ways to travel from La Paz, Bolivia to Cusco, Peru. What we chose to do was use the services of Bolivia Hop which is a hop-on-hop-off bus service that also had an optional stopover at Copacabana. These pictures tell the story of the first leg of that journey.

It was time to say our goodbye to La Paz, the highest capital city in the world. At over 3500m, built into a canyon, life in this city was an experience for us that has no known parallels.
It has only been about thirty minutes since we left La Paz. And we certainly did not miss the overcrowded scenery of that city. The opening vista was dramatic with very few buildings and homes seen.
A few hills in the horizon was a gentle reminder that we had not yet left the highlands. As the temperature slowly rose, the bus took us through a road that snaked down into the valleys. The appearance of a water body took me by surprise but I soon got to know that Lake Titicaca was so large that it would be with us through the entire journey.
Away from La Paz, life had a different rhythm and pace for the locals. We came across many faces that form the rural citizenry of this place. Faces that, like everywhere else, gave away signs of a harder life.
A boat? A movie prop? Someone's temporary dwelling? When I spotted what looked like a sea monster it got me excited. But there was no one to inquire with. Whatever it was, it was amusing in the way it greeted us, as if from some long forgotten time.
Soon, it was time to make our one and only crossing of the lake facilitated by a ferry system at San Pedro. We were moved to a smaller boat as our previous modes of transport were moved in a large ferry across. Soon, we would join them.
At the other end of the ferry, stood a statue of Manco Capac, welcoming us at the gateway to Lake Titicaca. Titicaca is part of many Incan legends, one of which claims that it was the origin of Manco Capac who went on to establish the Incan Empire.
As we navigated the plaza at the other end, walking in the general direction in which our bus was taken, we ran into many proud Cholitas. Cholitas have become a symbol of Bolivia itself after a dramatic turn of events in the very recent past.
A few minutes after boarding the bus again, we were descending. This time the expanse of water seemed larger, inflated only by the building up of enthusiasm. Lake Titicaca it was, but what we were looking at was territory that belonged to Peru. We would go to Peru eventually but not yet.
When the driver announced Copacabana, many among us thought we had reached our destination but it turned out to be yet another stop at a view point. The famous town looked picturesque from afar.
Soon, we had blended into the traffic and crowds below. A park in the town however had its own internal clock with people spending time at relatively slower speeds.
There at last! Copacabana. Our destination, for now, for a day, no, for a few fleeting moments before we picked ourselves up and carried on. But until then, it was time to dip our feet into the blue waters that birthed the Incan Empire.
