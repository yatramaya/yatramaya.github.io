The Amazon and its Tributaries
The Amazon and its Tributaries

Timeline : 2019 September
Peru South America Multi Day River Forest Nature

It was exactly how I had imagined it; perhaps better than that, because I remember sitting on that boat spellbound and self-aware that I had gone very quiet. The boat was navigating through the Maranon river in whose waters reflected the rising sun. The reflection moved ahead as we did ever so slowly, unhurried by the seeming speed that was implied by the engine itself which was the only thing I can remember as noise. Since it was daybreak, most people on the boat were still a bit sleepy despite the early morning bus-ride that got us to Nauta where we all boarded into a motorboat.

In about twenty minutes or so the sun had drifted as far as it would for this journey of ours. It had now painted the entire body of water ahead of us in a glowing orange, mimicking the real thing that was slowly rising up into the sky. The canvas on which this orange was painted was the meeting place of the Maranon and the Ucayali rivers. Our boat was making a turn now. From going with the flow, to against it. From downstream to upstream. The marriage of the two very wide rivers wasn’t like any other. I had a strange feeling of being held responsible for something. In this case, for the birth of one of the world’s longest rivers. We were making this large U-turn at the very place from where the river would be called the Amazon. I had yet another strange feeling; this time, a guilt of betrayal, or a tinge of jealousy perhaps, for some of the canoes and boats that were headed east for the day, to follow this geographical masterpiece to better conclusions through the settlements in Peru and then to the large country of Brazil, finally to drain somewhere far away into the sea.

But that had to be for another day. On this particular day we had already headed southbound, upstream the Ucayali. The Ucayali itself is a long river, its head waters somewhere in the South of Peru and traversing almost the entire country to meet the Maranon in the North. The boat slowed down almost instantaneously and the stress on the engine was felt via its hoarse cry. For the next few hours it would be like that, against all that water that was incessantly flowing in the other direction. The sun was now illuminating the backs of our heads. While Nauta was a blur - due to the fact that I was just getting used to the morning, combined with the unavoidable anxiety of a traveller who had to switch modes of transport - what I saw now on either side registered well. There were only a few homes visible and most of it in very small clusters. I wasn’t sure how deep this clusters ran inland but from what was visible, I could see that most homes had a canoe anchored right in front of their homes. The homes were basically huts-on-stilts, the heights of which are testimony to the time and experience of the people who lived there and their relationship with the river. Sometimes I saw a man or a woman with weather-beaten faces drying a produce here or fixing a net there. Sometimes we stared; not at each other of course, them at the moving boat and me at their lives. It was inspiring to say the least. I was clearly looking at the present and past at the same time. This was real and they were people cut off from a lot of what is today the modern world, their trade route being just this river and yet it was the past too for the human story, a past mostly forgotten by the largesse.

Just then, the boat switched off. Engine trouble is what I understood when the driver and usher decided to walk the aisle to the rear and of the boat and open that ubiquitous room which always has NO-ENTRY written on the door and which always makes me curious. Fume and stench filled the cabin when someone opened it. But apparently it wasn’t a big concern and we were moving again. I enjoyed the break though; with the engine shut off, the boat wobbled and moved freely in the Ucayali for a moment to take the experience into primitive realms. A thought crossed my mind; what if I got to experience the canoes? That would be nice, but I wasn’t sure if that can be arranged. The momentary silence also drew my attention to the birds that were about the water, the most interesting being the Black Skimmer which swooped down once in a while just above the surface of the water, positioning its lower beak under the surface and looking for something to feed on. There were other familiar birds too; Anhingas, Herons and Ducks. All of them on a feeding spree before the sun moved into harsher angles.

There was just one stop before reaching our destination. It was a largish place the size of which I would never fully grasp but by the amount of huts I could see and the fact that it merited a jetty. The boat moved in close and a few more people boarded. The time was about 11 in the morning and we were expected to be at Requina where our contact Jose would be waiting for us. It would be overly complicated to find Jose among a whole lot of people. But that wouldn’t be a problem because there were only two of us foreigners today on the boat which meant that we only had to stay put at the Jetty after getting off; he would find us. The river shrunk in width after our first stop but it was nevertheless very deep right at the centre. The sky that was dominating the landscape up until then was slowly giving way to taller and taller vegetation on the banks; a clear indication that as we progressed we could expect lesser of the clearings and more of the jungle. I sat in anticipation as the boat turned another corner and far ahead I saw yet another settlement, this time composed of a lot more buildings, roads and permanent structures; Requina, where another episode of our journey was to begin.
