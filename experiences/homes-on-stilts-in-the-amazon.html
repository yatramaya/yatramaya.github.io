Homes on Stilts in the Amazon

Timeline : 2019 September
Peru South America Multi Day River Forest Nature

The bare-bones dwelling on stilts appeared like creatures that could discreetly shy away inland when the river asserts its rights annually. I was excited like never before to step inside, as Jana (pronounced Yaana) explained the basics to us. Jana was a volunteer with the organization. She spoke with passion about the project; its mission and accomplishments. But mainly, she paused to speak about the mosquitoes when I inquired. I did not have an original inquiry. In fact I shouldn’t have bothered to even ask. Good insect life is perhaps the best indicator of the extent to which nature surrounds us and thereby its quality. This part of Amazon was far away from all else, and wild and wet enough to provide us all sorts of multi-footed companions; some flying, some crawling and some others humming about close to our ears. Mosquitoes seems to be an overly reductive term I was using for all sorts of life that surrounded us. As I slowly came to terms with the idea of sharing a largely open living area sort of space, part of which was also the bathroom, with beings that did not need makeshift doors to come in and go out, Jana reminded us that this was nothing compared to our actual treks into the forest itself the next day.

The structure had no walls to speak of, i.e. in a continuous sense and as we usually know it. Perhaps in an attempt to keep it airy or out of some habituated design sense, the walls were rows and rows of planks of wood that had more than an inch of space between them at many places. This also meant that the structure did not need official windows; although I recollect seeing them. Light managed to come in through all the crevices. From the ground floor a semblance of a staircase went steeply upwards to the mezzanine area which was a very small bedroom. It wasn’t much of a room, so to speak. The stairs simply ended in a landing area that had a small bed with a mosquito net around it. The walls in that area were slightly more closer to each other as if to acknowledge the fact that it was an area where people desired a bit more privacy. These structures were designed with a single person in mind or at best for two intimately close people. Most importantly, they were designed for living for the least amount of time, i.e. during the nights when it made sense for all of us to let go of our intrusive nature and head into our own nests and let the animals be. I did realize that we would be using it very little. The plan anyway was to spend most of daylight trekking through the jungle that was outside.

I looked at the floor below. Once the eyes adjusted and focussed through the panel gaps I could see the forest floor underneath. The area was dry at present because the Tapiche river was at least 30 feet away from the dwellings. It wouldn’t be so in the seasons when the rivers were wider and dominant around here in the Amazon basin. I wondered if it could ever happen that I would come here again in that season of abundance of water. I had heard many stories of that being an entirely different experience. I had also heard that an attempt to visit the Amazon in the wettest months came with its own risks; such as a situation where one would spend days or even weeks barely surviving in such dwellings unable to deal with the elements outside or have any activity. It wasn’t a risk I could afford despite the inherent temptation of an even closer communion with this jungle. Water, wetness, rain and fecundity somehow described the Amazon better, as if it were the fundamentals, not unlike the poles on which the platform of this structure rested. I do think a full Amazon experience would be to simply sit disabled and defeated by all that water - cold and withering, in a corner - as the same water acts as a driving force all around, recreating life in so many forms to keep this forests alive; the perfect example of me participating without participating.

In the nights that came later when sun went past the Andes, the river, its banks and all the trees blended into an undivided darkness, I tried to force myself to sleep, rolling around in a bed trying to find the perfect posture that would somehow align my tired spine with the handmade cot, textures of which that were oddly discernible even through the thin bed. My mind would crawl back each night down the stairs and out into the pathway that led down to the jetty, into the boat and down the river to the various spots that we had visited each day. I closed my eyes, an act that had no bearing on any sort of physical reality because it was pitch black nevertheless. The forest outside sang many tunes outside, each of which I tried to identify; tests that usually failed simply because I wasn’t that good at registering sounds but also because there were far too many and all of it arrived at the same time. At times I thought I heard sounds of the forest from within the structure. Insects or amphibians that had had found the panel gaps large enough to be deemed an invitation, were performing their act. Life, all of it, in the process of creating more life. That is all that was there to it. All this, the posturing, the sounds, the risks involved in making all this noise, the variations in calls, all of it had a singular purpose. Finding a mate and passing on the genes. I felt like the Amazon was a living embodiment of life and its purpose itself.
