Staying in a cavehouse at Goreme, Cappadocia
Staying in a cavehouse at Goreme, Cappadocia

Timeline : 2018 April
Turkey Asia Multi Day Culture People Historical

Goreme was a beautiful town in a valley beyond the vast, brown mountains and large smooth rocks that resembled endless mounds of mud. That morning, we were transferred to a mini-bus after an overnight journey in a comfortable, large bus. We had got used to the efficiency of this country, and the general goodness of people, so we only had to step out of the bus and step into the smaller bus, without having to fuss about our luggages, which were perfectly tagged and transferred promptly. Having traversed vast countryside, with probably breathtaking landscapes during our night journey, all lost to us in the darkness, I was itching to soak in every little detail that I could see out of my window during this short journey. The gentle caress of the morning sun warmed my cheeks and melted my laziness away. What looked like a vast desert initially, strewn with scant vegetation, on closer look were mountains of sandstone, a unique type of soft stone that defines the landscape around these parts. Everything eventually turns into a shade of brown, with fine dust floating around in the air.

Huge rock formations, in shapes of camels, umbrellas, elephants, that can only be limited or expanded by the viewer’s imagination stood majestically amidst this landscape. The road, a narrow streak of grey, like a messy strand of hair wound around the mountains, up one and down another. Then they appeared. Curious structures, with windows, that looked like sand castles we build at the beach - long, conical structures, seemingly hollow, dwellings of dwarf cavemen, fairy chimneys as they were called. Who would imagine carving out the insides of huge rock formations and decide to live in them? why would someone choose this natural formation for a home rather than building a house with bricks and wood? Is it the ingenuity of these people that stood out or their sheer laziness that made them live in these cones? Although it looked like miniature structures with crammed spaces initially, when they came closer, they were roomy enough for a small family. The winds are so ruthless around these parts, with the sand blown around, that it was only practical to choose a structure that already withstood the strength of the winds.

Just as I was wondering about these unique dwellings, our bus entered a small town with neatly built rows of homes and well laid out streets. Then we had arrived at our own cave, our dwelling for the next two days. We would experience first hand what it would feel to be a caveman. As we walked into the gate, our host, Farooq welcomed us warmly and showed us to our room. Although we were hesitant to occupy it, having arrived there way ahead of our scheduled arrival time, his warm smile immediately put me at ease. It was the usual check-in process, but the order in which he chose to do it and the manner in which he made us comfortable, made me feel like home. Without asking for IDs or making entries in the register first, he invited us to the breakfast, which he mentioned was complimentary, because, we had arrived at breakfast time! This hospitality was something I had not expected, but would never forget.

The spread was nothing short of spectacular, a typical sumptuous Mediterranean spread, with fresh fruits, olives, jams, jellies, freshly baked bread, juices, hot tea, with multitude of choices. And it was served personally by Farooq and his son, we were their guests. The warm sunny balcony with a view of the Cappadocia valley and the cold morning breeze was a perfect setting for this unforgettable breakfast experience. There was a freshly baked fruit cake too, which just arrived, warm, as we were eating. The delicious aroma of cinnamon, nutmeg filled the air as Farooq cut a piece for me. The smiles and gestures of our warm hosts and our shy smiles, repeated “tashkiyor edirim"s from us, was sufficient to keep the conversation going. Not knowing Turkish was a handicap, but somehow I did not feel that it came in the way of this wonderful breakfast experience. We all ate like one large happy family. Farooq’s wife sat on the next table, nibbling at a piece of cake, having the satisfaction of serving her guests and seeing them eat well. It is this hospitality that I very well recognized, that reminded me of home, where guests are treated as equivalent to God. Half way around the world, travelling with scant belonging, amidst unfamiliar climate and unrecognizable tongues, I eagerly accepted any gestures, scenes or sounds that would remind me of home. The Turkish culture was so unique, yet in so many ways similar to Indian culture.

Chatting with Farooq later, I asked him about his carpet collection, which decorated the walls of his homestay. Colourful, intricate, thick carpets that were handwoven with dyed woollen strands. I related to them, having seen many handcrafted and handwoven carpets back home. The plight of the handwoven carpets, with stiff competition from the machine made ones, with the artisans losing livelihood, everything seemed to be exactly same as what I had witness back in India. Farooq knew well about their woes and offered to help them by using his homestay to sell some of the carpets. We had deep conversations about the artisans, the craft, the effort put into each carpet, the economic situation, the onslaught of industrial processes eroding whatever was left of the handicrafts. It felt like I had found a long lost friend, with whom I could speak freely, knowing that he would understand me well, pick up strands of conversations right from the middle and take it forward, without having to explain a background, a premise, like how we generally need to do with someone who is unfamiliar with our culture. He expressed a desire to visit India again, having travelled there a couple of decades ago, and having fallen in love with the people, he wished to reconnect with that past someday. And likewise, somewhere deep in my heart, I wished too, to return, to Turkey, to Goreme and this quaint little fairy chimney, that I can call home.
