We still have not seen the Tapir
We still have not seen the Tapir

Timeline : 2019 August
Bolivia South America Half Day Forest Wildlife River Trekking

At 4am, I could see the faint light in the other tents. Braving the cold and in complete darkness with the help of a flash-light, I managed to crawl out of mine. The moonless sky shone with a million stars. Wearing our rubber boots our team of four started walking towards the forest. The cicadas seemed to cheer us on and follow us into the darkness as we moved in. The eerie ambience was complemented by a million other insects that were probably protesting as we walked through the maze of branches and roots. Using our flash-light we looked for any signs of owls in the canopy. The forest represented an entirely different being at night. We were intruders here in a realm ruled by other creatures; we were entering their well guarded territory. I was certain that not all of them will treat us kindly and let us trespass.

Soon, we found ourselves in a clearing and we were on the banks of the Tuichi. The river announced itself with the gentle sound of shallow water; a welcome replacement to the orchestra of the cicadas. I struggled to keep my foot on stable ground thanks to the rounded boulders of all sizes and shapes. The occasional sand-beds gave my feet the much needed break from the stone-walking. The sand also gave us a chance to look for footsteps of animals that might have treaded there earlier; according to Ricardo, a few minutes ago. He soon squared in on something; fresh criss-crossing tracks of deer and jaguar, which made me very excited. To simply know or even imagine that a Jaguar might be lurking around in the bushes, watching and waiting patiently for us to move ahead is something that makes these 4am patrols totally worth it.

There was also a larger and deeper footprint which gave an impression that the creature was bulky, clumsy and not too fast. This was of the Tapir, a weird elephant-faced furry animal that is unique to this ecosystem. Tapirs, when chased by Jaguars are known to run into the river and hide underwater exposing only their longish trunks to the surface. They can stay there for extended periods of time till the Jaguar gives up on the hunt and leaves. I wondered if the Jaguar deprived of its meal would take a passing interest in us, as we alternated between staring into the bushes and the river. Tapirs are also known to take dips in the cool waters to escape the flies that bite them. Perhaps that was all it was. Or perhaps it was nothing, and I was letting my imagination run wild.

We decided to stay there for a while and wait. We all sat on the riverbank in complete darkness, without a sound, watching the beautiful starry sky above tuning our ears to any sound of animal activity. After a while, I heard a whistle. It was unmistakable; a Tapir. It was nearby and Ricardo was excited. He returned the call with a whistle that mimicked the creature. The intention was draw it nearer. We heard the whistle again, this time softer. Ricardo returned it, yet again; this desperate attempt giving away his disappointment. Moments turned into minutes before we heard it again. This time it seemed from far away. It was too dark to know if it was even on our side of the river. Ricardo never gave up though till it all went silent.

After what seemed like ages, but in fact only a few minutes I felt a finger press against my shoulder. I ignored this initially duly noting it as an accident when someone moved about but soon realized that the finger belonged to Ricardo who was also whispering something. He must have spotted it now, I thought. “Agouti!”, he said. “What?” I asked lowering my voice. “Agouti, look,” he said. It was faint and it was extremely dark and all I could spot was the rear-end of an animal that if not for Ricardo’s prompt I would have mistaken to be anything between a hare or a pig. But it was larger than a hare and smaller than a pig. In fact, if it was brighter, it would be apparent that the Agouti, like the Capybara is a rodent. In a flash it was over, and we were all back in our poses, waiting for the Tapir. We continued to sit there by the Tuichi for another hour despite being robbed of an opportunity to see this unique creature but were also thankful for the little mercies such as a glimpse of another.
